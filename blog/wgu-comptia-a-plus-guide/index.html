WGU CompTIA A+ Certification: Study Strategy and Exam Tips

January 5, 2026 11 min read Exam Preparation

For many WGU IT students, CompTIA A+ is the first milestone that turns “I’m learning IT” into “I can do IT.” It’s a foundational certification built around real helpdesk workflows: troubleshooting hardware, networking basics, operating systems, and the day-to-day operational habits that keep systems stable and secure.

This guide is written specifically for WGU CompTIA A+ students who want a focused plan. You’ll get a clear strategy for both exams, how to handle performance-based questions (PBQs), and the exam-day tactics that reduce stress and improve your first-time pass odds.

If you’re mapping your entire degree journey, start with our WGU BSIT guide and our WGU certification guide. They help you see where A+ fits, what typically comes next, and how certifications can accelerate your timeline.

Why CompTIA A+ matters for WGU students

WGU is competency-based. The fastest students aren’t “lucky”—they’re intentional. A+ is a perfect example: when you treat it as skill-building (not memorization), you build a foundation that makes later coursework and certifications easier.

  • It builds WGU IT fundamentals: hardware, networking, security basics, and troubleshooting patterns you’ll use repeatedly.
  • It improves your speed: strong fundamentals reduce the time you spend stuck in later courses.
  • It’s helpdesk-realistic: many questions look like actual tickets and real-world symptoms.
  • It sharpens decision-making: the exam rewards “best next step” thinking, not trivia.
  • It sets up the “next cert” path: students often move from A+ into more specialized networking or security topics (depending on program).
Mindset shift: Don’t ask “What do I need to memorize?” Ask “What would I do if I saw this problem at work?” That’s the fastest way to pass the A+ exam WGU students struggle with.

One more benefit: A+ gives you a shared language. When you say “DNS issue,” “driver problem,” “port,” or “least privilege,” you’re speaking the language used by IT teams. That makes it easier to learn in cohorts, collaborate in study groups, and communicate with instructors.

Exam structure: Core 1 vs Core 2 (what you’re actually up against)

CompTIA A+ requires passing two exams from the same version: Core 1 (220-1201) and Core 2 (220-1202). Each exam can include up to 90 questions with a 90-minute time limit, and both include a mix of multiple-choice, drag-and-drop, and performance-based questions (PBQs).

Passing scores are commonly listed as 675 (Core 1) and 700 (Core 2) on a 900-point scale. The exact question mix varies, so plan to be strong across the objectives instead of chasing shortcuts.

What makes A+ hard (even for smart students)

A+ is “foundational,” but it’s broad. That breadth is the real challenge: the test expects you to make good choices across hardware, networking, OS tools, and security. You’ll see questions where two answers look correct, and the difference is a detail like best practice, safety, or least downtime.

  • It’s scenario-based: you’re often troubleshooting symptoms, not reciting definitions.
  • It’s practical: the exam expects real-world habits like documenting changes and verifying fixes.
  • It’s time-pressured: PBQs plus multiple-choice requires pacing, not perfection.

Core 1 (220-1201): hardware + networking + troubleshooting

Core 1 is heavily weighted toward hardware and troubleshooting. It’s also where many students feel the first wave of “port numbers and acronyms.” The reliable approach is to learn systems and symptoms—not isolated facts.

Core 1 domains are commonly listed as:

  • Mobile Devices (13%): device types, connectivity, and support basics
  • Networking (23%): cabling, wireless, IP basics, and network troubleshooting
  • Hardware (25%): components, peripherals, printers, and replacement decisions
  • Virtualization/Cloud (11%): core concepts and practical use cases
  • Hardware/Network Troubleshooting (28%): methodology and decision-making

Core 2 (220-1202): OS + security + procedures

Core 2 is where you prove you can support users and systems safely and consistently. It includes operating system workflows, security fundamentals, software troubleshooting, and operational procedures that show up in real IT jobs.

Core 2 domains are commonly listed as:

  • Operating Systems (31%): installation, configuration, common tools and features
  • Security (25%): controls, threats, and safe operational habits
  • Software Troubleshooting (22%): diagnosing and fixing common problems
  • Operational Procedures (22%): documentation, change control, professionalism
Important: Many students underestimate Core 2 because it “sounds familiar.” Don’t. Security and OS questions often include tricky wording and multiple “almost right” answers.

A practical study plan for CompTIA A+ WGU students

Here’s a realistic approach that works for most students—whether you’re trying to finish quickly or you’re starting from zero background. The goal is to turn study time into passing performance using feedback loops.

Step 1: pick your timeline (then protect it)

A+ is two exams, and momentum matters. A common pattern is:

  • Accelerated track: 2–4 weeks per exam with focused daily study
  • Foundation track: 6–8 weeks per exam if you’re building IT fundamentals from scratch

Pick a track based on your available hours per week. Then choose a specific exam date window. If you study without a target, the scope of A+ expands forever. If you study with a target, you learn to prioritize and you improve faster.

Step 2: build your “pass loop” (learn → test → fix gaps)

Most people fail because they study in a straight line: read, watch videos, hope for the best. Instead, use a loop:

  1. Learn: cover objectives with structured content (WGU resources + supplements).
  2. Test: take timed practice exams and PBQ-style drills.
  3. Fix gaps: review wrong answers and rebuild weak areas with short, targeted sessions.
  4. Retest: repeat until your performance is stable under time pressure.
Pro tip: Track mistakes in a simple “miss log” (topic + why you missed it + correct reasoning). It turns random practice into a measurable plan.

Step 3: use score targets instead of “I feel ready”

Readiness is not a mood. It’s a trend. A practical benchmark is: multiple full-length practice exams where you score consistently above your personal target, and you can explain answers in plain English.

  • Target consistency: not one high score, but repeatable performance over several attempts.
  • Target reasoning: you can explain why the correct answer is correct and why the distractors are wrong.
  • Target timing: you can finish comfortably without rushing the final 20–30% of the test.

Step 4: schedule the exams like a two-part project

Because A+ is two tests, treat it like a two-part project with a shared foundation. The most common mistake is finishing Core 1 and then taking a long break. Instead, plan the second exam while you’re still studying for the first.

Momentum rule: If you can keep Core 2 within 2–4 weeks after Core 1, you retain troubleshooting habits and score higher with less total study time.

Two sample schedules (choose one)

2-week sprint (per exam): best if you already have some experience and can study daily.

  • Days 1–3: fast coverage of all objectives; build a baseline and identify weak zones.
  • Days 4–10: deep focus on weak zones + daily question sets + PBQ drills.
  • Days 11–13: timed full practice exams + review miss log.
  • Day 14: light review and exam.

6-week steady plan (per exam): best if you’re new to IT or have limited daily hours.

  • Weeks 1–2: foundations (hardware/network for Core 1; OS/security for Core 2) + short quizzes.
  • Weeks 3–4: objective coverage + troubleshooting practice + targeted notes.
  • Week 5: timed practice exams + PBQ drills + gap repair.
  • Week 6: mixed review + final full practice + exam scheduling and readiness checks.

Using WGU resources effectively (and what to add)

WGU students often have access to structured materials and practice tools. The mistake is assuming one tool alone is enough. Your goal is coverage + repetition + realism.

Start with the course outline (it’s your objective map)

Use the course outline and objectives as your map. If you’re new to IT, resist the urge to skip “easy” sections. In A+, basic concepts are the foundation for troubleshooting questions, and troubleshooting is where most of the points are.

CertMaster Learn + CertMaster Practice: how to use them without getting stuck

If your program includes CertMaster tools, treat them like a guided curriculum and diagnostics, not a complete strategy.

  • Learn: move through objectives with short sessions and frequent recall checks.
  • Practice: don’t just “take quizzes”—review wrong answers until you can explain why the correct option is correct.
  • Retain: re-quiz on weak objectives every few days, not just once.
  • Simulate: do timed blocks so test-day pacing is normal, not new.

Many students also add a second resource for explanation and variety (for example: structured video lessons and multiple timed practice exams). That variety reduces the risk of memorizing a single question bank instead of learning the content.

Reality check: If your practice scores look good but you can’t explain the reasoning out loud, you’re not ready yet. That gap shows up on the real exam—especially in PBQs.

Don’t ignore “soft skill” objectives

Operational procedures can feel obvious, but they’re easy points if you learn them properly. A+ expects professional habits: verifying fixes, documenting changes, respecting policy, and choosing the safest option when multiple fixes exist.

If you have no IT background, build a mini lab

CompTIA often recommends hands-on support experience before attempting A+. You can still pass without a job in IT, but you’ll need hands-on practice. A simple home lab makes abstract topics real:

  • PC basics: identify components and understand what they do (CPU, RAM, storage, PSU, motherboard).
  • Network basics: log into your router, identify devices, and practice troubleshooting Wi‑Fi issues.
  • OS practice: create users, change permissions, install/uninstall apps, and use system tools.
  • Command-line comfort: run basic commands and interpret output (don’t panic when you see a terminal).

High-yield topics: what to master for Core 1 and Core 2

If your study time is limited, you need a priority list. This section is designed as a “focus filter” so you spend less time on low-impact trivia and more time on what actually drives your score.

Core 1 high-yield checklist

  • Troubleshooting steps: identify the problem, establish theory, test, plan and implement, verify, document.
  • Hardware basics: what each component does, when to replace vs repair, and common failure symptoms.
  • Printers: common issues, maintenance kits, connectivity problems, and symptom-based diagnosis.
  • Networking fundamentals: IP basics, Wi‑Fi troubleshooting, cable types, and common services/ports.
  • Cloud/virtualization basics: what virtualization is, why it’s used, and basic cloud concepts.

Core 2 high-yield checklist

  • OS tools and workflows: installation, updates, users/groups, permissions, and common utilities.
  • Security fundamentals: safe configuration choices, common attacks, and basic incident response thinking.
  • Software troubleshooting: symptoms, isolation steps, and clean fixes (without breaking other things).
  • Operational procedures: documentation, change control, professionalism, and safety.

OS tools worth practicing before test day

Core 2 questions often assume you know where to look first. Practice using these tools so you can answer quickly and confidently:

  • Task Manager: identify resource spikes, stuck processes, and startup impact.
  • Device Manager: spot driver issues, disabled devices, and hardware conflicts.
  • Disk Management: understand partitions, drive letters, and volume status.
  • Event Viewer: find error patterns by timestamp and event type.
  • System configuration and updates: know the safe order: update, reboot, verify.

Security “best choice” thinking

When two answers seem right, CompTIA typically rewards the option that reduces risk and follows policy: least privilege, verified updates, safe defaults, and clear documentation.

  • Least privilege: give the minimum access needed, not “admin to make it work.”
  • Safer first action: validate the issue and use the least disruptive fix first.
  • Prevent recurrence: patch, educate, and document after resolving the immediate issue.
Fast confidence builder: Every time you learn a new concept, ask “What symptom would I see if this fails?” and “What’s the safest first check?” That’s how A+ questions are written.

Ports and protocols (how to learn them without suffering)

You don’t need an encyclopedic list, but you do need the common ones. A practical approach is to group ports by use:

  • Web: basic web and secure web
  • Email: sending and retrieving email, secure variants
  • Name/addressing: DNS and DHCP basics
  • Remote access: remote management and file sharing basics

Instead of memorizing a spreadsheet, turn each port into a tiny story: “If this service is blocked, what breaks?” That way, even if you forget a number, you can often reason your way to the correct answer by what the service does.

For example, if users can connect to Wi‑Fi but can’t reach websites, your first mental checks should be: is the device getting a valid IP address, is DNS working, and is the browser being redirected by a proxy or security filter. When you attach ports to symptoms, you learn faster and you retain longer.

PBQ strategy: how to stop PBQs from wrecking your score

Performance-based questions (PBQs) feel intimidating because they’re not “pick one option.” They simulate real tasks. The good news: PBQs are predictable in the skills they test, even if the exact scenario changes.

How PBQs are commonly structured

  • Ticket-based troubleshooting: identify likely causes from symptoms and apply the right fix.
  • Network basics: connect devices logically, choose correct settings, or diagnose connectivity.
  • Command-line tasks: run the right tool, interpret output, and make a decision.
  • Security fundamentals: choose safer configurations and recognize bad practices.

A simple PBQ workflow you can reuse

  1. Restate the goal: what outcome is required?
  2. List constraints: what are you not allowed to do?
  3. Check basics: power, connections, correct settings, correct account/permissions.
  4. Change one thing: isolate variables; don’t “shotgun” random changes.
  5. Verify: confirm the fix using evidence, not vibes.
  6. Document: even mentally—what did you change and why?

PBQ time management that actually works

A strong approach is to triage PBQs:

  1. Skim all PBQs early (don’t solve them yet).
  2. Answer fast multiple-choice questions to build points and confidence.
  3. Return to PBQs with a clear time budget and a calm mind.
PBQ rule: If you can’t explain your steps clearly, you don’t fully understand the workflow. Practice until you can narrate your reasoning.

Exam-day tips: reduce stress and protect your points

Even well-prepared students can lose points from avoidable mistakes: rushing, overthinking, and time mismanagement. Treat test day like a process.

Before you start

  • Plan a distraction-free block: no meetings, no errands, no “I’ll squeeze it in.”
  • Use a stable setup: reliable internet, charged laptop or power cable, and a quiet room.
  • Do a quick warm-up: 10–15 minutes reviewing your miss log and top weak areas.
  • Have a pacing plan: know how you’ll handle PBQs (triage first, then return).

During the exam

  • Read like a technician: identify the symptoms, constraints, and what the question is really asking.
  • Eliminate options: remove wrong answers first; then choose the best remaining option.
  • Watch for “almost right” traps: many answers are plausible but miss a key detail.
  • Protect your time: don’t sink 8 minutes into one question if 12 easier ones are waiting.
  • Don’t over-fix: some questions want the safest first step, not the most advanced tool.
Common failure pattern: students spend too long on PBQs and rush the final third of the exam. Your best scores come from balanced pacing.

If you don’t pass: a smart retake plan (without burning weeks)

First: failing an exam is not a character flaw. It’s feedback. The key is to respond like an IT professional: diagnose the failure, isolate the causes, and apply a targeted fix.

Step 1: turn your results into a checklist

Use your score report to identify weak domains and the specific types of questions you missed. Then create a short remediation plan that focuses on the highest-impact objectives first.

Step 2: rebuild fundamentals, not just questions

Retakes go wrong when students repeat the same study method that didn’t work. If you missed troubleshooting and networking, you need workflows and practice—not more reading.

Step 3: prove readiness with consistency

Schedule the retake only after you see consistent performance on timed practice exams and you can explain your reasoning. If you can’t explain your choices, you’ll repeat the same mistakes under pressure.

Fast reset: Spend one session building a “Top 20 mistakes” list from your miss log. That single list often improves your score more than random extra study.

After you pass: how to leverage A+ (and keep accelerating)

Once you pass both exams, don’t let the win fade into “on to the next thing.” Use A+ to create momentum:

  • Update your resume and LinkedIn: add A+ immediately and list 3–5 workflows you can now demonstrate.
  • Build a “ready for helpdesk” story: highlight troubleshooting, documentation, and security habits.
  • Keep your notes: your A+ troubleshooting patterns will carry forward into networking and security.
  • Plan what’s next: use the WGU certification guide to map your next certification targets.

If your goal is fast graduation, build a repeatable system: plan → execute → test → refine. That’s the same approach that helps with WGU courses, papers, and proctored exams. Frequently Asked Questions

Many WGU IT programs include CompTIA A+ as an embedded certification, but requirements vary by program version and degree plan. Confirm the exact certifications for your program in your Degree Plan and course materials.

A common range is about 2–4 weeks per exam with focused daily study, while students new to IT often plan 6–8 weeks per exam. Your timeline depends on your background, available hours, and how quickly you can consistently score well on practice tests.

Most students take Core 1 first because it builds hardware, networking, and troubleshooting fundamentals that make Core 2 easier. If you already have helpdesk experience and feel confident in hardware, you can choose either order—but keep your momentum and schedule the second exam right away.

CertMaster can be a solid primary resource for coverage and diagnostics, but many students benefit from adding a second explanation resource and multiple timed practice exams. The safest approach is to judge readiness by consistent practice scores and your ability to explain answers, not by completing a single course path.

PBQs are hands-on simulations where you troubleshoot or configure something (like a helpdesk ticket, command-line task, or network issue). Prepare by practicing common workflows: interpreting symptoms, checking basics first, using the right tools, and documenting a clear fix.

You should know the most common ports and what they do (for example, web, secure web, email, DNS, remote access). CompTIA questions often test whether you can connect a symptom to the right service, not whether you memorized an exhaustive list.

Don’t panic—treat the score report as a study roadmap. Work with your course instructor or mentor to build a remediation plan, then retake when your practice scores are consistently above your target. Retake voucher policies can vary, so confirm your program’s current process before scheduling.
