WGU Program Mentor Guide: Build a Winning Relationship

Updated January 2025 | 9 min read

Your WGU program mentor can be your greatest asset or biggest frustration depending on how you manage the relationship. Unlike traditional universities where you might rarely see an advisor, WGU's mentor model means you'll be talking to yours every single week.

This guide shows you how to make that relationship work for you - whether you're looking to accelerate through your degree or just need help staying on track with a busy schedule.

Reality Check: Your mentor's job is to keep you progressing. They're measured on student completion rates. This means a good mentor is invested in your success - but it also means you need to manage the relationship to get what YOU need.

What Your Program Mentor Actually Does

First, let's clarify what your mentor does and doesn't do:

Mentor Responsibilities

  • Weekly check-ins - Required phone/video calls to discuss progress
  • Course planning - Help you sequence courses and plan your term
  • Enrollment management - Add courses, handle term extensions, enrollment holds
  • Resource connection - Connect you with tutors, course instructors, writing center
  • Motivation and accountability - Keep you on track toward graduation
  • Problem solving - Help navigate WGU policies and systems

What Mentors DON'T Do

  • Teach course content - That's what Course Instructors (CIs) are for
  • Grade assessments - Evaluators handle that
  • Guarantee approval for acceleration or transfers
  • Provide extensions on assessment deadlines (that's your CI)

Key Distinction: Your mentor handles the administrative side of your education. For academic help - understanding concepts, exam prep, assignment feedback - you need your Course Instructor. See our OA exam tips and pre-assessment guide for study strategies.

Nailing Your First Mentor Call

Your first call sets the tone for your entire relationship. Come prepared to establish yourself as a serious, self-directed student.

Before the Call

  • Review your degree plan and understand the courses required
  • List any transfer credits you're bringing in
  • Have your work schedule and study availability ready
  • Know your target graduation timeline
  • Prepare questions about acceleration policies

Topics to Cover

  1. Your background: Work experience, prior education, why you chose WGU
  2. Your goals: Target completion date, career objectives
  3. Your schedule: When you'll study, how many hours per week
  4. Acceleration: Ask about the process to add extra courses
  5. Communication preferences: Best times to call, email vs. phone

Sample Script: "I'm coming in with [X] transfer credits and plan to dedicate [X] hours per week to studying. My goal is to complete this program in [X] terms. I'm particularly interested in understanding the process for acceleration once I demonstrate good progress."

Making Weekly Calls Work for You

Weekly mentor calls are mandatory at WGU. Miss too many and you risk enrollment holds. But these calls don't have to be a burden - here's how to make them efficient:

Be Prepared

Have your progress report ready: courses completed, current course status, next steps. A 10-minute call beats a 45-minute rambling session.

Schedule Strategically

Book calls during your commute or lunch break. Many students do calls while driving (hands-free) to maximize time efficiency.

Take Notes

Document any commitments your mentor makes. "You said you'd add that course by Thursday" holds them accountable.

Ask for Less Frequent Calls

Once you've demonstrated consistent progress (usually after 1-2 terms), ask about bi-weekly calls. Many mentors will agree.

The Ideal Call Structure (10-15 minutes)

  1. Update (2 min): "I completed [X], currently working on [Y], planning to finish by [date]"
  2. Requests (2 min): "I need you to add [course] / approve [assessment] / connect me with [resource]"
  3. Questions (3 min): Any blockers or concerns
  4. Next steps (2 min): Confirm action items and next call time

Getting Mentor Support for Acceleration

If you want to accelerate your degree, your mentor is both gatekeeper and advocate. Here's how to win them over:

Prove Yourself First

Don't ask for 4 extra courses on day one. Complete your first course ahead of schedule, then:

Sample Script: "I finished [Course Name] two weeks ahead of schedule and passed the OA on my first attempt. I'd like to add another course to stay challenged. What's the process for that?"

Present a Plan

Mentors love organized students. Come with:

  • Your proposed course sequence
  • Estimated completion dates for each course
  • How you'll balance multiple courses
  • Your study schedule and available hours

Handle Pushback Professionally

If your mentor resists acceleration:

Don't: Get confrontational or go over their head immediately

Do: Ask specific questions - "What metrics do I need to hit?" "What's the typical timeline for acceleration approval?" "What concerns do you have about my plan?"

If you consistently demonstrate success and still face unreasonable resistance, you can request a mentor change (more on that below).

Communication Best Practices

Email Tips

  • Use clear subject lines: "Request: Add C779 to Current Term" beats "Question"
  • Be specific: Include course codes, dates, exactly what you need
  • Create paper trails: Follow up verbal conversations with email confirmation
  • Give deadlines: "If possible, I'd like this resolved by Friday so I can start the weekend"

When Your Mentor is Unresponsive

  1. Send a follow-up email after 48 hours
  2. Try text message if you have their number
  3. Call during their office hours
  4. If still no response after a week, contact Student Services

Document Everything

Keep a simple log of:

  • Call dates and key discussion points
  • Promises made (by you and your mentor)
  • Any issues or concerns raised
  • Approvals received

This protects you if there are ever disputes about what was agreed upon.

When and How to Change Mentors

Sometimes the relationship just doesn't work. Valid reasons to consider a change:

  • Consistent unresponsiveness (multiple days to reply)
  • Unreasonable resistance to acceleration despite good performance
  • Scheduling conflicts that can't be resolved
  • Communication style mismatch
  • Mentor lacks knowledge of your specific program

How to Request a Change

  1. Try to resolve issues first - Have a direct conversation about what's not working
  2. Contact Student Services - Call or use the support chat
  3. Be specific but professional - Focus on fit, not blame
  4. Request specific qualities - "I need someone familiar with IT certifications" or "I work best with someone who supports acceleration"

Sample Request: "I'd like to request a mentor change. While I appreciate [current mentor's] support, I'm finding that our communication styles aren't well-aligned. I'm looking for a mentor who has experience with accelerating students and is familiar with the [specific program] requirements."

Types of Mentors You'll Encounter

Mentors have different styles. Knowing what you're working with helps you adapt:

The Cheerleader

Traits: Very positive, lots of encouragement, may not push hard
Best for: Students who need emotional support
Watch out for: May not proactively offer acceleration opportunities

The Administrator

Traits: Strictly by-the-book, follows all policies precisely
Best for: Students who want clear rules and consistency
Watch out for: May be inflexible on edge cases

The Coach

Traits: Goal-oriented, pushes you to perform, data-driven
Best for: Accelerators and ambitious students
Watch out for: May be too intense for students needing flexibility

The Hands-Off

Traits: Minimal contact, quick calls, lets you do your thing
Best for: Self-directed students who hate micromanagement
Watch out for: May not catch issues before they become problems

Frequently Asked Questions

What does a WGU program mentor do?

Your WGU program mentor is your primary point of contact throughout your degree. They help with course planning, degree progression, enrollment issues, and general support. They are not subject matter experts but can connect you with course instructors for content questions.

How often should I talk to my WGU mentor?

WGU requires weekly check-ins with your mentor, typically via phone call. Most calls last 10-30 minutes. You can also communicate via email and text between calls. Accelerating students may request less frequent calls once they demonstrate consistent progress.

Can I change my WGU mentor?

Yes, you can request a mentor change if your current mentor isn't a good fit. Contact Student Services or your current mentor to initiate the change. It's best to give specific reasons for the request to help WGU match you with a better fit.

What if my mentor won't let me accelerate?

First, ask specifically what metrics you need to demonstrate. Complete courses quickly and successfully to build a track record. If you're consistently performing well and still facing resistance, you can request a mentor change to someone more supportive of acceleration.

Can I skip mentor calls?

No - weekly calls are mandatory. Missing calls can result in enrollment holds that prevent you from taking assessments. If you need to reschedule, let your mentor know in advance. Some students have negotiated bi-weekly calls after demonstrating consistent progress.

Key Takeaways

  • Your mentor is an administrator, not a tutor - use Course Instructors for academic help
  • Come to calls prepared - organized students get more mentor support
  • Document everything - keep records of commitments and agreements
  • Build trust before asking for extras - prove yourself, then request acceleration
  • Change mentors if needed - it's your education, you deserve good support
