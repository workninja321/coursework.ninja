WGU Special Education Degrees: Become a Licensed SPED Teacher

January 3, 2026 10 min read Degree Guides

Special education teachers are in high demand across the United States, with the Bureau of Labor Statistics projecting steady growth in SPED teaching positions through 2032. WGU's special education degree programs offer an online, competency-based path to becoming a licensed special education teacher, designed specifically for working adults who want to make a difference in the lives of students with disabilities. This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about WGU's SPED programs, from program options and clinical requirements to state licensure and career outcomes.

WGU special education programs at a glance

WGU's Teachers College offers two bachelor's degree programs specifically focused on special education. Both programs lead to initial teacher licensure and are designed to prepare you for working with students who have mild to moderate disabilities across K-12 settings.

  • B.A. Special Education (Mild-to-Moderate) - Leads to K-12 special education licensure
  • B.A. Special Education and Elementary Education (Dual Licensure) - Leads to both special education K-12 and elementary education licensure

WGU lists its Teachers College programs as accredited by CAEP and AAQEP. WGU is also regionally accredited by NWCCU, which supports broad recognition of your degree.

Key advantage: WGU's competency-based model means you can accelerate through material you already know while spending more time on challenging concepts. This is particularly valuable for career changers who may have relevant experience.

Comparing the two special education programs

Choosing between the mild-to-moderate program and the dual licensure program depends on your career goals and the state where you plan to teach. Here's a detailed comparison:

Student teaching Full-time, supervised student teaching (WGU cites 12–20 weeks) Full-time, supervised student teaching (WGU cites 12–20 weeks)

B.A. Special Education (Mild-to-Moderate)

The BASPMM program is designed for candidates who want a K–12 special education license in states that offer a single special education license focused on mild-to-moderate exceptionalities. In practice, this often means learning how to support students who can access the general curriculum with the right accommodations, small-group instruction, progress monitoring, and targeted interventions.

Availability note: WGU lists this program as not available in Delaware, New Jersey, and American Samoa. WGU also notes that students in Colorado, Louisiana, and Massachusetts may need to pass content exams in an area other than special education before a special education license will be issued. Always confirm current eligibility and testing requirements for your state before enrolling.

B.A. Special Education and Elementary Education (Dual Licensure)

The dual licensure program prepares you for certification in both special education and elementary education. This option provides maximum career flexibility, allowing you to work as a general education elementary teacher, a special education teacher, or in inclusion settings that blend both roles.

Note that dual licensure availability depends on your state. Not all states offer dual certification pathways, so verify your state's options before choosing this program.

What “mild-to-moderate” means in practice

WGU describes these tracks as preparing you for mild-to-moderate special education needs. In most districts, that typically translates into supporting students in inclusive classrooms, resource rooms, and intervention settings with skills like:

  • Reading and math interventions using structured, evidence-based approaches
  • Progress monitoring and data-based instructional decisions (MTSS/RTI)
  • IEP development and service delivery (goals, accommodations, documentation)
  • Behavior support using positive, proactive strategies
  • Assistive technology and accommodations to remove barriers to learning
Important: Special education licensure categories and endorsements vary by state. If your goal is a moderate-to-severe endorsement (or a different SPED pathway), verify your state's requirements and WGU eligibility before enrolling.

What you will study

WGU's special education curriculum integrates research-based concepts from the Science of Learning and Science of Reading. The program builds your expertise in both foundational teaching skills and specialized special education competencies.

Core special education coursework

  • Foundations of special education - History, legislation (IDEA, Section 504), and ethical practice
  • Assessment and evaluation - Formal and informal assessments, progress monitoring, data-driven instruction
  • IEP development and implementation - Writing legally compliant IEPs, setting measurable goals, documenting progress
  • Behavior management - Functional behavior assessments (FBAs), behavior intervention plans (BIPs), positive behavioral supports
  • Differentiated instruction - Adapting curriculum, Universal Design for Learning (UDL), accommodations vs. modifications
  • Collaboration and communication - Working with families, general education teachers, related service providers, and administrators
  • Transition planning - Preparing students for post-secondary life, employment, and independent living

Science of Reading emphasis

WGU's newly redeveloped curriculum places strong emphasis on evidence-based reading instruction. This is particularly important for special education teachers, as reading difficulties are the most common reason students qualify for special education services. You'll learn structured literacy approaches, phonemic awareness instruction, and intervention strategies for struggling readers.

Clinical experience and student teaching

WGU's special education programs require substantial in-person classroom experience. This is not optional - you cannot earn licensure without completing these clinical requirements.

Clinical experience progression

  1. Virtual simulations - Video-based observations and virtual classroom scenarios early in the program
  2. Early clinical experience - Initial hours in authentic classroom settings, observing and assisting
  3. Advanced clinical experience - Increased responsibility, co-teaching, and lesson delivery
  4. Student teaching (Demonstration Teaching) - Full-time, supervised classroom experience

Student teaching requirements

WGU's teacher licensure programs require full-time, supervised student teaching. WGU describes Student Teaching as a minimum of 12–20 weeks, but the exact length and requirements can vary by program and by state, so plan ahead and confirm details with WGU and your state's licensure office.

  • Full-time, supervised student teaching (WGU describes it as a minimum of 12–20 weeks)
  • Clinical experiences before student teaching (early and advanced clinical phases)
  • Full-time commitment during student teaching (plan time off work and childcare early)
  • Background check and fingerprint clearance before entering the classroom
  • Supervision by both a host/mentor teacher and a WGU clinical supervisor
Plan ahead: Student teaching is a full-time commitment. You'll follow your placement school's calendar and work the same hours as your mentor teacher. WGU strongly recommends not working during this period.

State licensure requirements

Teacher licensure is always state-specific. WGU publishes state eligibility information for its teacher preparation programs, but requirements (tests, fingerprints, timelines, and reciprocity rules) vary. Before you enroll, check your state's requirements and confirm WGU eligibility for your state of residence.

Common state requirements

  • Background checks and fingerprinting - Required in all states, sometimes at multiple points
  • Basic skills tests - State-specific exams required before (or as part of) student teaching
  • Content exams - State-required tests for special education and/or elementary education
  • Pedagogy/professional knowledge exams - State-required tests covering teaching methods and practice
  • State application and fees - Vary by state, typically $50-$200

What WGU highlights for licensure-track students

WGU notes that candidates seeking initial teacher licensure must pass a state-specific basic skills test as a prerequisite to Student Teaching (registering and paying for the test is the student's responsibility). WGU also requires a criminal background check prior to entering the classroom for clinical experiences and Student Teaching.

Admission requirements

WGU's School of Education has additional requirements for licensure-track programs. Two big ones to plan for early are the basic skills test requirement and the background check/fingerprinting requirement.

  • Basic skills test: WGU notes a state-specific basic skills test is required as a prerequisite to Student Teaching (registering and paying for the test is the student's responsibility).
  • Background check and fingerprinting: WGU must receive clearance before Student Teaching, and processing can take time depending on your state.
  • Age requirement: WGU notes you must be at least 18 to begin the application process or participate in Preclinical Experiences and Student Teaching.

Exact requirements vary by state and can change, so confirm details with WGU and your state's teacher licensure office.

Transfer credits can significantly reduce your time to degree. WGU evaluates transcripts from regionally accredited institutions and accepts credits for courses that meet their competency requirements.

Real-world tip: Plan for a "student teaching season" financially and logistically. Reddit and student forums are full of stories where candidates underestimated how hard it is to work full-time during Student Teaching. Even if you plan to work, assume you may need time off.

Cost and financial aid

WGU's flat-rate tuition model charges per six-month term rather than per credit. This makes acceleration financially rewarding - the faster you complete competency units, the lower your total cost.

Tuition structure

  • Undergraduate School of Education tuition (terms beginning on or after September 1, 2025): $3,825 per 6-month term
  • E-Books & Resources Fee: $200 per 6-month term
  • Total per term: $4,025 (before any state-specific expenses)
  • Other costs to plan for: fingerprinting/background checks and state-required exams

Financial aid options

  • Federal financial aid - Pell Grants, Direct Loans, and other federal programs
  • WGU scholarships - Scholarship opportunities for eligible students
  • State grants and scholarships - Many states offer funding for teacher candidates
  • Employer tuition reimbursement - Some school districts offer tuition support for paraprofessionals pursuing teaching degrees
Budget tip: In addition to tuition, plan for state-required testing fees and background check costs. If you’re employed by a school district, ask about paraprofessional-to-teacher programs or tuition reimbursement.

Career outcomes and job market

Special education teachers are in high demand in many districts, and local need can be especially strong in high-need areas. National outlook varies by specialty and grade level, but the Bureau of Labor Statistics continues to track special education roles as a major category within K–12 education.

Career paths with a SPED degree

  • Resource room teacher - Pull-out instruction for students needing intensive support
  • Inclusion specialist - Co-teaching in general education classrooms
  • Self-contained classroom teacher - Teaching students who need full-day specialized instruction
  • Learning specialist - Diagnostic and intervention roles
  • Transition coordinator - Preparing high school students for post-secondary life
  • Behavior specialist - FBA and BIP development across settings

Salary expectations

The Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook lists $64,270 as the 2024 median pay for special education teachers. Salaries vary significantly by state, district, and experience level:

  • Entry-level positions typically start between $40,000-$50,000
  • Experienced teachers in high-paying districts can earn $80,000+
  • Additional stipends often available for high-need positions
  • Master's degrees and National Board Certification can increase salary

Sample timeline for working adults

Completion time varies based on your transfer credits, study pace, and clinical scheduling. Here's a realistic timeline framework:

  1. Before enrollment: Verify state licensure requirements, gather transcripts for transfer credit evaluation
  2. Terms 1-2: General education and foundational education courses (if needed)
  3. Terms 2-3: Core special education coursework, early clinical experiences
  4. Terms 3-4: Advanced methods courses, advanced clinical experience
  5. Final term: Student teaching and remaining assessments

Students with significant transfer credits and strong academic backgrounds have completed the program in as few as 18–24 months. However, student teaching is time-based and full-time. Even if you accelerate coursework, you’ll still need to complete the required in-classroom student teaching experience (WGU cites 12–20 weeks, depending on your state and program).

Acceleration strategies

WGU's competency-based model rewards students who can demonstrate mastery quickly. Here are proven strategies for accelerating through the program:

  • Maximize transfer credits - Submit all transcripts and request a thorough evaluation
  • Take pre-assessments immediately - Identify your knowledge gaps before diving into course materials
  • Use the rubric as your guide - For performance assessments, the rubric tells you exactly what evaluators expect
  • Schedule clinical experiences early - Background checks and placement coordination take time
  • Study consistently - 15-20 hours per week minimum, more if you want to accelerate
  • Connect with your mentor - WGU mentors can help you sequence courses strategically
Reality check: While coursework can be accelerated, clinical requirements cannot. Plan your timeline around student teaching (WGU cites 12–20 weeks, depending on your state and program), not around how fast you can complete online coursework.

Common challenges and how to overcome them

Balancing work and study

Most WGU students work full-time while completing their degrees. The flexibility is real, but so is the workload. Create a study schedule and protect that time. Many successful students wake up early, study during lunch, or use weekends for focused work.

Clinical scheduling conflicts

If you work, you'll need to arrange time off or adjust your work schedule during student teaching. Start this conversation with your employer early. Some students transition to part-time work or take unpaid leave during student teaching.

State licensure exam preparation

Don’t underestimate state-required licensure exams. Requirements vary by state, and WGU provides preparation resources, but it’s smart to budget time for dedicated exam prep and practice tests so testing doesn’t delay your student teaching timeline.

IEP writing skills

Writing legally compliant IEPs is a skill that takes practice. Pay close attention to IEP coursework and ask for feedback during clinical experiences. This is one of the most important competencies you'll develop.
