WGU Network+ Certification: Networking Fundamentals Exam Prep

January 5, 2026 11 min read Exam Preparation

If you’re in an IT program at Western Governors University, you’ll see one pattern again and again: certifications aren’t just “nice to have” — they’re part of the degree plan. And when it comes to networking fundamentals, WGU Network+ (CompTIA Network+) is one of the most important milestones.

This guide is built for busy students who want to pass confidently and move on fast. You’ll get a clear picture of what Network+ tests, which topics matter most, and how to build a repeatable study system — even if you’re balancing work, family, and a tight term timeline.

We’ll focus on what actually moves the needle for Network+ WGU success: domain priorities, subnetting drills, troubleshooting methodology, and performance-based question (PBQ) readiness — plus a realistic 3–6 week plan you can execute in small daily sessions.

For a broader exam mindset and pacing strategies you can apply across your term, see How to Pass WGU Objective Assessments. The test-taking logic translates well: measure your baseline, focus on weak areas, and practice under realistic timing.

What Is CompTIA Network+ (and Why WGU Includes It)

CompTIA Network+ is a vendor-neutral certification that validates you can understand, implement, and troubleshoot networks in real-world scenarios. It’s not a “memorize definitions” exam — it expects you to recognize symptoms, choose the right tools, and apply networking concepts under time pressure.

In many WGU IT programs, WGU CompTIA Network+ is included because it builds a foundation for later courses and certs. Network+ connects the dots between the “what” (protocols and hardware) and the “why” (design choices and troubleshooting decisions).

Network+ also has a practical career angle: it’s one of the fastest ways to move from “I can build a PC” into “I can support networks.” It’s relevant for help desk roles aiming to level up, junior network technicians, and anyone moving toward systems, cloud, or security roles where networking fundamentals are non-negotiable.

If you’re still deciding on a degree path, start with our WGU BSIT guide and compare it with role-focused programs like WGU Network Operations. Understanding the roadmap helps you prioritize the right certifications at the right time.

Where Network+ Fits in WGU (Course Codes and Expectations)

Depending on your catalog year and program, Network+ may appear under different course codes. Many students see it as D325 or C714. The exact labeling matters less than the outcome: you’re working toward the exam voucher and a passing score.

One reason Network+ feels “different” from a typical WGU Objective Assessment is that certification exams demand applied judgment. You’ll often see scenario-style questions and PBQs where you must interpret output, choose the right next step, and validate a fix.

Another difference is scope: Network+ covers a lot of ground. You don’t need expert depth in every topic, but you do need coverage and you need confidence with the highest-frequency skills (subnetting, troubleshooting, and common services).

Pro Tip: Before you begin serious study, open your course materials and confirm the exam version and objective list you’re expected to follow. Build your notes around those objectives so you don’t drift into “interesting but not tested” content.

Network+ Exam Overview (N10-009, Format, and Scoring)

The current CompTIA Network+ exam version is N10-009. If you’ve seen older discussions about N10-008, treat them as historical context — objectives change over time and you want your study plan aligned to what you’ll actually be tested on.

Here are the core test-day facts you should know:

  • Time limit: 90 minutes.
  • Question count: up to 90 questions total.
  • Question types: a mix of multiple-choice and Performance-Based Questions (PBQs).
  • Passing score: 720 on a 100–900 scale.

That structure dictates how you prepare. Your goal isn’t to “know everything about networking.” Your goal is to reliably answer the types of questions CompTIA asks, within the time limit, using the mental models they reward.

Important: Network+ rewards precision. If you don’t know the difference between similar concepts (like DNS vs DHCP, or TCP vs UDP behavior), the exam will force you to pick the best answer — not just a plausible one.

Domain Breakdown: What to Focus On First

Network+ is organized into five domains. The weights matter because they tell you where your study time has the highest return:

  • Networking Concepts: 23%
  • Network Implementation: 20%
  • Network Operations: 19%
  • Network Security: 14%
  • Network Troubleshooting: 24% (highest)

Most students underestimate troubleshooting. But the weighting is a strong hint: if you’re trying to accelerate, you can’t treat troubleshooting as an afterthought. You should practice troubleshooting as a process — not as random fact recall.

Networking Concepts (23%): Build Your “Mental Model”

This domain is the foundation. When you understand these ideas, the rest becomes easier:

  • IP addressing and subnetting basics (CIDR, host ranges).
  • How routing differs from switching (and what each device does).
  • Common protocols and what problems they solve.
  • Wireless standards and fundamentals (band, coverage, interference).

Network Implementation (20%): Know What “Good Setup” Looks Like

Implementation questions often ask you to choose the right option for a requirement. You want to recognize patterns:

  • How to segment networks logically (subnets, VLAN-like thinking).
  • What cabling choices imply (distance, reliability, interference).
  • What a correct address assignment looks like (static vs DHCP use cases).
  • How to interpret and validate basic configurations.

Network Operations (19%): Monitoring, Documentation, and Change Control

Operations content is easy to ignore, but it’s common on exams because it reflects real work:

  • Why documentation matters (diagrams, IP plans, baselines).
  • Operational habits: verify, monitor, and document outcomes.
  • Recognizing symptoms of overload or failure before users complain.
  • Understanding when to escalate and what evidence to include.

Network Security (14%): High-Yield, Not Deep

This isn’t a Security+ exam, but Network+ expects you to understand basic network defense thinking:

  • Why segmentation reduces blast radius.
  • Why secure protocols replace insecure ones (SSH vs Telnet, HTTPS vs HTTP).
  • How misconfigurations create security issues.
  • How to think about least privilege and safe remote access.

Network Troubleshooting (24%): The Score Multiplier

This domain is where you can gain the most points. Troubleshooting questions usually test your ability to:

  • Identify likely causes from symptoms.
  • Choose the right tool or next diagnostic step.
  • Confirm whether a fix actually worked.
  • Document or escalate appropriately.

Even if you’re comfortable with concepts, you need reps on applied decision-making. That’s where PBQ practice and timed drills become important.

A Practical 3–6 Week Study Plan for Network+ WGU

Everyone wants the fastest possible pass — but the fastest path is the one that avoids retakes. A retake can cost you days or weeks of momentum, and it often forces you into more structured remediation.

Use this plan as a framework and adjust based on your baseline knowledge.

Week 1: Build a Map of the Objectives

  • Create a one-page outline of the five domains and subtopics.
  • Identify your weak spots early (common ones: subnetting, routing/switching logic, troubleshooting workflows).
  • Start a “miss list” notebook: every time you miss a concept, write the topic and the correct reasoning.

Your goal in Week 1 is speed of mapping, not mastery. By the end of the week you should be able to explain — in simple language — what each domain is about and what a typical question might look like.

Week 2: Implementation + Operations (Make It Real)

This is the phase where many students shift from passive studying to competence. Don’t just read; apply:

  • Practice interpreting simple network diagrams (who talks to whom, and where failure could occur).
  • Drill common protocols and ports until they’re automatic.
  • Practice basic troubleshooting commands and what their output implies.
  • Do short subnetting drills daily (even 5–10 minutes).
Pro Tip: Treat ports like vocabulary. Don’t memorize a number in isolation — always pair it with a purpose ("What problem does this service solve?").

Week 3: Troubleshooting + PBQ Practice

Now you move from “learning” into “exam readiness.” Your goal is to answer scenario questions quickly and consistently.

  • Run timed sets of troubleshooting questions.
  • Practice the 7-step troubleshooting method until the sequence is reflexive.
  • Do PBQ-style drills: identify the problem, choose the next action, validate the fix.
  • Keep updating the miss list and review it daily.

Weeks 4–6 (as needed): Patch Weaknesses and Build Speed

Most students fall into one of two categories:

  • You’re close but slow: you understand concepts, but timed pressure causes mistakes.
  • You’re fast but shaky: you can move quickly, but knowledge gaps cause wrong answers.

In either case, the fix is the same: targeted practice with feedback. Use your miss list and spend most of your time on the topics that keep reappearing. If you’re accelerating, this is where you stop “learning new” and start “repeating what’s tested.”

Hands-On Practice Without Fancy Labs

WGU students often ask if they need expensive lab equipment. You don’t. Network+ requires thinking and troubleshooting more than it requires gear. A simple setup can get you a long way:

  • Use your home router and two devices (laptop + phone) to observe DHCP behavior and IP details.
  • Practice explaining what an IP address, mask, gateway, and DNS server do — and what breaks when each one is wrong.
  • Run basic commands (ipconfig, ping, tracert, nslookup) and interpret results.
  • Draw a simple network diagram for your home setup and label the “points of failure.”

That last one sounds basic, but it trains exactly what the exam wants: translating a scenario into a network model you can troubleshoot.

Subnetting: The Make-or-Break Skill

Subnetting is where many Network+ attempts go sideways — not because it’s impossible, but because students avoid it. On the exam, subnetting can show up directly (CIDR, VLSM, host ranges) and indirectly (routing decisions, network segmentation, troubleshooting).

Network+ expects you to calculate:

  • CIDR notation: what a /27 or /29 implies.
  • Network and broadcast addresses for a given IP and mask.
  • Valid host ranges and how many hosts fit.
  • VLSM choices (choosing different masks for different needs).

Subnetting Confidence Checklist

If you can do these quickly, subnetting stops being scary:

  • Convert a /24, /26, /27, /28, /29, /30 into “hosts per subnet.”
  • Given an IP and CIDR, identify the network address and broadcast address.
  • Given required host counts, choose the correct mask (VLSM mindset).

A Simple Way to Train Subnetting Speed

  1. Choose a small range of masks: start with /24 through /30.
  2. Practice three outputs for each: network address, broadcast address, usable host range.
  3. Time yourself (even 5 minutes per day adds up).
  4. Increase difficulty with VLSM once /24–/30 feels easy.
Important: Subnetting isn’t just “math.” It’s pattern recognition. You don’t need to write a full page of calculations — you need fast, repeatable steps.

Master the 7-Step Troubleshooting Method

CompTIA explicitly tests a structured troubleshooting workflow. Know the sequence, and also know what each step looks like in practice:

  1. Identify the problem (gather information, question users, identify symptoms).
  2. Establish a theory of probable cause (start with obvious, then move deeper).
  3. Test the theory to determine the cause (confirm or reject).
  4. Establish a plan of action to resolve the problem and identify potential effects.
  5. Implement the solution or escalate as needed.
  6. Verify full system functionality and implement preventive measures.
  7. Document findings, actions, and outcomes.

On Network+ exam tips discussions, the theme is consistent: the exam loves “next best step” questions. If you have the troubleshooting method internalized, these questions become easier because you’re selecting the step that matches the scenario’s current state.

A Quick Troubleshooting Mindset That Works on PBQs

When you see a scenario, ask yourself:

  • Is this a name resolution problem (DNS) or an address assignment problem (DHCP)?
  • Does connectivity fail locally, on the LAN, or only to the internet?
  • What single test would reduce the uncertainty the most?

Ports and Protocols: Memorize the High-Yield Set

Ports show up constantly in Network+ questions and PBQs. You’re expected to recognize the protocol, the port, and the practical scenario where it applies.

Start with this high-yield list:

  • FTP: 20/21
  • SSH: 22
  • Telnet: 23
  • SMTP: 25
  • DNS: 53
  • DHCP: 67/68
  • HTTP: 80
  • HTTPS: 443
  • SNMP: 161/162
  • RDP: 3389

How to Memorize Ports Without Cramming

Instead of flashcards that only contain “number → name,” add a one-line scenario. Example:

  • 53 (DNS): “Users can’t reach websites by name, but IP works.”
  • 67/68 (DHCP): “New device can’t get an IP address automatically.”
  • 443 (HTTPS): “Secure web access; certificate and TLS issues can break connectivity.”

This style of study is especially effective for WGU Network+ because it turns memorization into recognition — and recognition is what exam questions reward.

PBQs: How to Handle Performance-Based Questions

PBQs are the part of the exam that makes people nervous — but they’re also where you can gain points if you practice the right way. PBQs are simulated scenarios: you might configure something, match outputs to tools, or troubleshoot a broken network segment.

PBQs frequently reward these habits:

  • Read the task first, then scan the interface. Don’t wander.
  • Identify constraints. What must be true when you finish?
  • Use basic tools confidently. Know what commands like ping, tracert, nslookup, and ipconfig tell you.
  • Work from layer to layer. Start with the simplest connectivity checks and move upward.
Pro Tip: PBQs often feel hard because they’re unfamiliar — not because the networking is advanced. Treat each PBQ like a mini troubleshooting ticket and follow a process.

PBQ Time Strategy

If PBQs appear early, don’t panic. You have options:

  • If you’re strong at PBQs, do them first while your brain is fresh.
  • If PBQs stress you out, do a quick scan, flag them, and build momentum on multiple-choice first.

The best approach is the one that keeps you calm and accurate. Accuracy beats speed if speed causes mistakes.

Common Network+ Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

Most Network+ failures aren’t caused by “not being smart enough.” They come from predictable mistakes in how people prepare. If you avoid the traps below, your first-attempt pass rate goes way up.

1) Studying Without a Tight Objective Map

Network+ covers a huge surface area. If your studying isn’t anchored to the exam objectives your WGU course points you to, it’s easy to drift into topics that are interesting but not tested. That drift feels like progress, but it’s actually lost time.

Fix: Keep a one-page “objective map” and tag every weak area you find during practice. When you miss a question, write down the objective behind it. This keeps your work laser-focused.

2) Skipping Subnetting Until the End

Subnetting shows up in direct questions and it also shows up indirectly in routing, segmentation, and troubleshooting. If you leave it for last, you’ll feel constant anxiety every time you see an IP scenario.

Fix: Start subnetting drills in Week 1 and do them daily. Short daily reps beat a single long cram session.

3) Memorizing Ports Without Understanding Use Cases

Knowing that DNS is “53” is less useful than knowing what a DNS problem looks like in real life. The exam tests recognition, not trivia.

Fix: Attach a one-line scenario to every port you memorize (for example: “Name doesn’t resolve, but IP works” for DNS). Then practice choosing the next diagnostic step.

4) Treating Troubleshooting as Random Guessing

CompTIA’s troubleshooting questions are often “next best step” questions. If you guess the next step without a process, you’ll lose points on questions that you could have solved with structure.

Fix: Learn the 7-step troubleshooting method and use it as your default playbook. If you can clearly identify what step you’re on, you can usually eliminate wrong answers quickly.

5) Practicing Untimed (Then Freezing on Exam Day)

Untimed practice is great for learning, but Network+ is a timed performance. If you never practice under time pressure, you’ll run out of time or second-guess yourself.

Fix: Add timed drills in Week 3. Start small (10–15 questions) and build up. You’re training calm speed.

Important: Network+ is a breadth exam. You don’t need deep expert knowledge in every topic, but you do need consistent coverage across domains.

Troubleshooting Tools Cheat Sheet (What They Tell You)

Network+ PBQs and scenario questions frequently expect you to understand a few basic tools and what their output implies. You don’t need to memorize every flag — you need to know what each tool is for and what conclusion you can reasonably draw.

Ping

What it tests: basic IP connectivity and reachability. If ping fails, you could be dealing with wrong addressing, routing issues, or filtering/firewall rules. If ping succeeds, it’s evidence the path is reachable at least at a basic level.

Tracert / traceroute

What it tests: where traffic stops. This can help you see whether the problem is inside the local network, at a gateway, or further upstream. It’s especially helpful when “the internet is down” but only certain destinations fail.

Nslookup

What it tests: name resolution. If an IP works but a hostname doesn’t, that’s a classic DNS signal. If nslookup returns errors or unexpected records, the fix is often DNS configuration rather than “network is broken.”

Ipconfig

What it tests: the device’s local configuration (IP address, subnet mask, gateway, DNS). If a device has an incorrect mask or gateway, it can look like “network failure” even when the network is fine.

Pro Tip: When you see a scenario, think in layers: configuration (ipconfig) → reachability (ping) → path (tracert) → name resolution (nslookup). This sequence mirrors how real network technicians work.

Practice Exams: How to Know You’re Ready

Practice questions are useful only if you review them correctly. The real benefit isn’t the score — it’s the feedback loop:

  1. Take a timed set.
  2. Review every missed question and write the reason you missed it.
  3. Map the miss back to a domain and a concept.
  4. Target that concept with focused practice, then retest.

When you’re preparing for networking certification WGU requirements, your goal is consistency. If your score swings wildly between attempts, you need more structured review. If your score is stable and improving, you’re building durable understanding.

A Simple “Readiness” Rule of Thumb

Don’t chase a perfect score. Chase a stable one. If you can take multiple timed sets and your results are consistently solid — and your mistakes are small (not huge concept gaps) — you’re close.

Final 72-Hour Checklist

The last few days should be about consolidation and confidence, not frantic learning. Focus on the highest-yield skills and the most common failure points.

  • Rehearse your subnetting process until it’s fast and calm.
  • Review the high-yield ports list with scenario prompts.
  • Recite the 7-step troubleshooting method from memory.
  • Do at least one PBQ-style drill where you interpret results and choose next steps.
  • Take one timed mixed set and review misses using your miss list.
  • Prepare your testing setup and reduce exam-day friction.
Pro Tip: If you’re tired, your accuracy drops. A clean pass often comes from good sleep, a simple plan, and avoiding last-minute panic studying.

Exam Day Checklist (Reduce Stress, Increase Accuracy)

Even well-prepared students sometimes fail because of avoidable exam-day issues: distractions, time management, or avoidable mistakes on PBQs.

Before You Start

  • Confirm your testing requirements in advance (camera, ID, environment rules, allowed items).
  • Make sure your device and internet connection are stable.
  • Have a simple plan for time: don’t spend too long on early questions.
  • Decide ahead of time how you’ll handle PBQs (first vs later).

During the Exam

  • Use flags strategically: if a question is taking too long, flag it and move on.
  • Read for “best” not “true”: many answers are plausible. Pick the best fit for the scenario.
  • Manage time in chunks: avoid spending 5 minutes on a single early question.
