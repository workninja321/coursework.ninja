WGU Teaching Degrees: Become a Licensed Teacher Online

December 21, 2025 10 min read Degree Guides

WGU teaching degrees give working adults a flexible, competency-based route into the classroom. The key is matching the right program to your state licensure requirements and planning field experience early. This guide explains licensure vs non-licensure tracks, WGU's published clinical and student teaching requirements, and how to build a realistic timeline to get licensed.

WGU teaching degrees at a glance

WGU offers online teacher licensure programs designed to lead to certification in any of the 50 states, with requirements that still vary by state. Coursework is online, but licensure programs include in-person classroom experience.

  • Online, competency-based coursework with flexible pacing
  • Licensure programs can lead to certification in all 50 states (state rules apply)
  • In-person clinical experience and full-time student teaching are required

Licensure vs non-licensure: choose the right path

If your goal is to teach in a K-12 classroom, you need a licensure track. WGU also offers non-licensure education degrees (for example, Educational Studies) that do not lead to a teaching license.

Tip: Pick your target state first, then match your degree to that state's licensure pathway.
  • Licensure programs: Built for initial teacher certification and include clinical experience and student teaching.
  • Non-licensure programs: Focus on education roles outside classroom licensure.

State licensure requirements you must plan for

WGU notes that licensure requirements are state-specific and often include a background check plus testing. Depending on your state and program, you may need:

  • Background checks or fingerprinting
  • Basic skills exams
  • Subject-matter content exams
  • Pedagogy exams (Praxis is used for some programs)

Confirm the exact requirements for your state before you enroll so you can schedule exams and paperwork early.

Clinical experience and student teaching

WGU requires real classroom experience. Students complete clinical experience before student teaching. WGU lists a minimum of 65 hours of clinical experience and at least 60 days of student teaching. WGU also notes that student teaching is full-time and typically lasts 12 to 20 weeks, depending on state and placement requirements.

Plan ahead: Student teaching is full-time and must be completed on a school schedule. Build your work and family plan early.

How placement works and how to avoid delays

Placements depend on district availability and state rules. Start early to avoid gaps in your timeline:

  1. Start background checks and fingerprinting as soon as your state allows.
  2. Confirm the school calendar for your placement district.
  3. Keep consistent communication with your mentor about deadlines.
  4. Plan for a full-time teaching schedule during student teaching.

Which WGU teaching degree fits your goals?

Choose the program that matches your current education level and licensure goals:

  • No prior bachelor's degree: A bachelor's licensure program is typically the starting point.
  • Already hold a bachelor's: A master's-level licensure program may be the fastest route.
  • Already licensed: Consider advanced degrees that build specialization or leadership skills.

Sample timeline for working adults

  1. Before enrollment: Verify your state requirements and expected exams.
  2. Term 1: Complete core education coursework and any transfer credit evaluations.
  3. Term 2: Finish methods courses and complete required clinical experience hours.
  4. Final term: Complete full-time student teaching and finish remaining assessments.

How to finish faster without risking licensure steps

  • Transfer eligible credits to reduce general education requirements.
  • Use a consistent weekly study schedule to move quickly through competency units.
  • Schedule state exams early so scores arrive before student teaching.
  • Align your student teaching with district calendars to avoid delays.

Career outcomes and next steps

Licensure programs prepare you for K-12 classroom roles, while non-licensure programs can open doors to education support, training, or curriculum roles. If your goal is classroom teaching, keep licensure steps and placement timing as your top priorities.
