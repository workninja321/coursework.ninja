WGU Teaching Degrees: Become a Licensed Teacher Online

December 21, 2025 8 min read Degree Guides

WGU teaching degrees are built for working adults who want an online, competency-based path into education. The key is choosing the right degree track for your goal: licensure if you plan to teach in a public K-12 classroom, or non-licensure if you want education roles that do not require a state teaching license. This guide breaks down the pathways, explains WGU's published clinical and student teaching requirements, and gives you a clear planning framework so you can map your timeline before you enroll.

WGU teaching degrees at a glance

WGU's Teachers College offers online programs designed to prepare students for licensure, with requirements that still depend on your state. WGU states that its licensure programs can lead to certification in any of the 50 states, but each state sets its own rules and exam requirements. This makes planning essential from day one.

  • Online, competency-based coursework with flexible pacing
  • Licensure programs available across all 50 states (state-specific rules apply)
  • In-person classroom experiences are required for licensure programs

Licensure vs. non-licensure: choose the right track

If your goal is to teach in a public K-12 classroom, you need a licensure program. WGU's Educational Studies bachelor's programs are non-licensure and do not include student teaching, meaning they do not lead to a teaching license. These programs are best for students who want education-focused careers outside traditional public-school licensure.

Quick rule: If your state requires a teaching license for the role you want, select a WGU licensure program.
  • Licensure programs: Lead to teaching certification in your state and include clinical experience and student teaching.
  • Non-licensure programs: Provide education knowledge without student teaching or a teaching license.

State licensure requirements you must plan for

WGU notes that licensure requirements vary by state. Many states require a background check and teaching exams. WGU also explains that licensure pathways commonly include basic skills exams, subject-matter tests, and pedagogy exams (Praxis is a common example).

  • Background checks and fingerprinting
  • Basic skills or entry exams
  • Subject-area content exams
  • Pedagogy exams (Praxis is used in many states)

Your state sets the exact sequence and minimum scores, so confirm your requirements early to avoid delays during your final term.

What you will study in a licensure program

While the exact courses vary by program and grade level, licensure tracks typically focus on practical classroom readiness. Expect coursework that builds both teaching skill and instructional decision-making.

  • Lesson planning and standards-based instruction
  • Assessment design and data-informed teaching
  • Classroom management and student engagement
  • Differentiation and inclusive teaching strategies
  • Educational technology and digital learning tools

How assessments work at WGU

WGU is competency-based, so you move forward when you demonstrate mastery. Most courses use a mix of objective assessments (exams) and performance assessments (projects and lesson-planning tasks). The fastest path is to use rubrics as a checklist and focus your study on any weak domains you identify early.

If you prefer structure, build a weekly plan that alternates reading, practice, and assessment prep. Many students accelerate by scheduling a pre-assessment early, then concentrating on the areas where they scored lowest.

Clinical experience and student teaching requirements

WGU requires in-person, supervised classroom experience for licensure programs. According to WGU, clinical experiences include a minimum of 65 hours of in-classroom work before student teaching. Student teaching itself is a full-time, supervised classroom experience with a minimum of 60 days. WGU notes that student teaching is a full-time commitment and typically runs 12-20 weeks, depending on state and placement requirements.

Plan ahead: WGU highlights that student teaching is full-time and recommends that students do not work during this period so they can focus on the classroom experience.

WGU also emphasizes that student teaching includes supervised observations and evaluations. Expect a mentor teacher to evaluate your progress and provide midterm and final feedback as part of the licensure recommendation process.

Placement planning: what to expect

Placements must align with your program's subject area and grade level. WGU's placement guidelines explain that student teaching must occur in an approved, fully accredited school setting, and WGU does not waive or shorten the placement requirement based on prior experience. You should expect to follow the host school's calendar and work the same hours as your mentor teacher.

  1. Start early: Background checks and school approvals can take time.
  2. Align schedules: Your placement is tied to the school calendar.
  3. Meet state rules: Some states require longer than the 60-day minimum.

Licensure readiness checklist

Use this checklist to ensure nothing slows you down in your final term:

  • Confirm state licensure exams and required passing scores
  • Schedule background checks and fingerprinting early
  • Align student teaching dates with your employer and family calendar
  • Map course completions to clinical experience deadlines
  • Set aside time for lesson planning and observation documentation

Which WGU teaching degree is right for you?

Use your current education level and licensure goals to decide which program fits best:

  • No prior bachelor's degree: A bachelor's licensure program is usually the starting point.
  • Already hold a bachelor's degree: A master's-level licensure program may be faster.
  • Already licensed: Consider non-licensure master's or specialized education programs for advancement.

Transfer credits and pacing strategy

If you are transferring credits, request an official evaluation before you build your final plan. This helps you identify which general education courses are already complete and which content areas still need to be covered. With WGU's competency-based model, pacing is flexible, but you must still align your academic progress with clinical experience deadlines.

  • Use transfer credit to clear general education requirements early
  • Finish pedagogy and methods courses before clinical placement
  • Keep a buffer term in case exam or placement timelines slip

Acceleration is possible, but licensure steps are fixed in time. Treat exams and placements as hard deadlines, and then move coursework around them rather than the other way around.

Sample timeline for working adults

Every student's pace is different, but a simple outline helps you see the steps that must happen in sequence.

  1. Before enrollment: Verify your state licensure requirements and required exams.
  2. Term 1: Complete core education coursework and transfer credits if available.
  3. Term 2: Finish methods courses and required clinical experience hours.
  4. Final term: Complete full-time student teaching and remaining assessments.

How to finish faster without risking licensure steps

  • Transfer general education credits to reduce overall course load.
  • Use a weekly study schedule to move quickly through competency units.
  • Schedule required exams early to avoid end-of-term bottlenecks.
  • Coordinate with your mentor and placement team well in advance.

Common mistakes that slow students down

  • Waiting too long to start background checks and exam registrations
  • Underestimating the time required for full-time student teaching
  • Not aligning placement dates with school calendars
  • Skipping rubric reviews for performance assessments

Career outcomes and next steps

Licensure programs prepare you for K-12 classroom teaching roles, while non-licensure programs can lead to instructional support, training, curriculum, or education-adjacent careers. Graduates commonly pursue roles such as elementary teacher, middle or high school teacher, or special education teacher depending on their program focus. Non-licensure degrees can support careers in instructional design, corporate training, or education program coordination.
