WGU Project+ Certification: IT Project Management Exam Guide

January 5, 2026 9 min read Exam Preparation

For a lot of WGU students, CompTIA Project+ is the “different” certification: it’s not a networking exam, not a security exam, and it doesn’t care whether you can configure a switch. It’s about how work moves from idea to delivery in a real organization—requirements, stakeholders, constraints, schedules, risk, change control, and governance.

If you’re in an IT degree path like the WGU BSIT or another program that includes industry certs, Project+ is often the point where students realize: this is not a vocabulary test. The exam is scenario-driven and rewards process thinking—especially around Agile/Hybrid delivery and change management.

This guide is written for WGU students who want to pass Project+ (PK0-005) efficiently. You’ll get a clear breakdown of what the exam tests, how it typically maps to WGU coursework, a practical 2–3 week sprint plan, and high-yield “trap” areas that show up again and again.

What is WGU Project+ (CompTIA PK0-005)?

“WGU Project+” usually means the CompTIA Project+ certification exam. The current version is commonly referred to as PK0-005. It’s an entry-to-mid level IT project management certification designed for technical professionals who need to run projects as part of their role—without being full-time project managers.

For WGU students, Project+ is attractive for three reasons:

  • It’s directly relevant to real work: scope changes, stakeholder communication, schedules, and risk happen in every IT job.
  • It strengthens your degree outcomes: it’s one more proof point on your resume alongside your WGU coursework.
  • It’s commonly “Good for Life”: Project+ is widely described as a certification that does not expire like some other certifications.
Quick mindset shift: If a question asks what you should do “next,” the right answer is often analyze, communicate, or follow the process—not “jump straight to implementation.”

How Project+ fits into WGU (and why it matters for acceleration)

In many WGU IT programs, Project+ maps to D324: Business of IT – Project Management. Your exact mapping can vary by program and over time, so treat this as guidance and verify inside your WGU degree plan.

The reason this certification matters for WGU acceleration is simple: cert courses are pass/fail milestones. Passing the certification is the fastest way to clear the course. If you’re planning your term or stacking courses for a push, it helps to understand how Project+ compares to other common IT certs in your program. If you want that bigger-picture view, see WGU IT Certifications: Complete Guide to Certs Included in Your Degree.

Project+ can also act like a “bridge” course. It teaches you to think in frameworks that show up everywhere:

  • Process discipline: defining scope, tracking work, managing quality, and closing projects cleanly.
  • Stakeholder and governance awareness: compliance, approvals, change control boards, procurement.
  • Agile fluency: modern delivery models, ceremonies, and artifacts.
Important: Don’t treat Project+ like a “read the book once” course. The exam is heavy on scenario logic, and small wording changes can flip the correct answer.

Project+ exam format: what to expect on test day

Here are the key exam details you should build your plan around:

  • Time limit: 90 minutes.
  • Question count: up to 90 questions.
  • Question types: multiple-choice plus performance-based questions (PBQs).
  • Passing score: 710 on a 100–900 scale.

The biggest mistake students make is studying “wide” instead of “deep.” Project+ is not trying to trick you with obscure facts. It’s trying to see if you understand the order of operations in project management and whether you can recognize the best tool, document, or action in a scenario.

Project+ domains (PK0-005) and how to study them

Project+ is usually described in four main domains. The percentages below are a practical way to prioritize your time:

  • Project Management Concepts (33%): methodologies, roles, organizational structure, and core concepts.
  • Project Life Cycle Phases (30%): initiation through closing, including planning and execution behaviors.
  • Tools and Documentation (19%): schedules, charts, documents, and artifacts used to manage projects.
  • Basics of IT and Governance (18%): compliance, risk, procurement, and IT context (including cloud).

Domain 1: Project Management Concepts (33%)

This domain is where “Project+ is easy” turns into “Project+ is wordy.” You’ll see scenarios about selecting the right delivery approach and understanding authority and decision-making in different organizations.

  • Waterfall vs Agile vs Hybrid: know when each is appropriate and why.
  • Organizational structures: functional, matrix, and projectized (and the PM’s authority in each).
  • Agile roles and artifacts: Scrum roles, backlogs, and what ceremonies are for.

Domain 2: Project Life Cycle Phases (30%)

Think of this domain as “what happens when,” and “which document proves it.” The exam loves asking which artifact authorizes a project, what happens before execution, and what the PM should do when something changes midstream.

  • Initiation: the project charter authorizes the work and identifies the sponsor.
  • Planning: scope decomposition (WBS) and turning scope into a realistic schedule.
  • Execution: managing people, conflict, and communications while producing deliverables.
  • Closing: validation, lessons learned, and archiving documentation.

Domain 3: Tools and Documentation (19%)

This is where you pick up “easy points” if you train your pattern recognition. You don’t need to become a scheduling expert; you do need to know what each tool is used for and what it looks like in a scenario.

  • Gantt charts: timelines and schedules.
  • PERT / network diagrams: dependencies and path analysis.
  • Critical path: the longest path with zero float, and what delays it.
  • Fishbone diagrams: root cause analysis.
  • Burndown vs burnup: how Agile teams visualize work remaining vs completed work.

Domain 4: Basics of IT and Governance (18%)

Governance questions are often about constraints: regulatory requirements, security and privacy expectations, procurement rules, and the realities of modern IT projects (including cloud migrations and remote teams).

  • Compliance as scope: regulations and standards can create non-negotiable requirements.
  • Procurement and vendor risk: what changes when you outsource or use cloud services.
  • Governance and approvals: who needs to sign off and why.

WGU D324 fast-track approach: what to do first

If you want to pass Project+ quickly inside WGU, treat it like a small project with clear deliverables: understand the exam domains, practice the scenario logic, and prove readiness with consistent practice results. The fastest path is rarely “read everything.” It’s focus + repetition + review.

1) Start with a baseline and build your personal gap list

On day one, your goal is to find out what you already know and what you don’t. Even if you don’t feel ready, start by sampling questions across all four domains. Don’t worry about the score—your output is a list of weak areas and unfamiliar terms.

  • Make a gap list: capture every unfamiliar artifact, chart, role, or formula.
  • Tag it by domain: governance misses get studied differently than scheduling misses.
  • Turn gaps into flash rules: short if/then statements are perfect for Project+ (example: “CCB approval happens before implementation”).

2) Use WGU-provided tools strategically (not endlessly)

Many WGU students report having access to practice platforms and content that include tools like CertMaster Practice, Wiley/Sybex-style test banks, and video libraries. The winning strategy is to use these resources to diagnose and reinforce—not to grind for 10 hours a day.

Here’s a simple way to use practice content efficiently:

  1. Do a focused set of questions in one domain.
  2. Review every miss and write a one-sentence rule (what you should do next, or which tool/document applies).
  3. Re-test that same domain a day later, then move on.
High ROI habit: Keep a “trap answer” list. Every time you choose an answer that feels reasonable but is wrong, write down why it was wrong. Project+ repeats traps.

3) Plan your attempt like a deliverable (especially if you’re accelerating)

When you’re accelerating, the biggest risk is spending extra weeks “almost ready.” Set a decision point. If your practice performance is stable (not spiky) and you can explain why the right answers are right, schedule the exam and execute.

Project+ isn’t about perfection. It’s about process thinking. You can miss a handful of obscure items and still pass if you consistently choose the most correct, process-aligned action in scenarios.

Documents and tools you must recognize (the easy points)

Project+ has a lot of vocabulary, but you don’t need to memorize definitions in isolation. You need to recognize which artifact belongs to which phase, and what problem it solves. If you train this well, you’ll pick up points quickly—especially in the Tools and Documentation domain.

Must-know documents

  • Project charter: authorizes the project and names the project manager.
  • Scope statement and requirements: what is included and what is out of scope.
  • WBS (work breakdown structure): breaks scope into work packages that can be scheduled and estimated.
  • Schedule: tasks, durations, dependencies, and milestones (often visualized in a Gantt chart).
  • Communications plan: who gets what updates, how often, and in what format.
  • Risk register: identified risks, impact/likelihood, owners, and mitigation plans.
  • Change request: the formal mechanism to request and evaluate changes.
  • Lessons learned / closure artifacts: what worked, what didn’t, and what gets archived.

Must-know visual tools and charts

  • Gantt chart: timeline view of tasks and milestones.
  • Network diagram / PERT: dependency view that supports critical path thinking.
  • Kanban board: workflow visualization and WIP control.
  • Burndown chart: work remaining in a sprint over time.
  • Burnup chart: work completed versus total scope (helpful when scope changes).
  • Fishbone diagram: root cause analysis (causes grouped by category).
Common mistake: Students memorize what a chart is called, but not what decision it supports. Project+ asks for the decision.

Scenario playbook: how to pick the “most correct” answer

Project+ questions often present four answers that look plausible. The correct answer usually aligns with a professional project manager’s priorities: protect the baseline, follow governance, communicate early, and document decisions.

Use the “process filter”

When you’re stuck between two options, run this quick filter:

  • Does it require approval first? If yes, any “do it now” answer is a trap.
  • Is there missing information? If yes, analyze or gather requirements before acting.
  • Is this a stakeholder conflict? Communication and alignment usually come before changes to scope.
  • Is it a quality issue? Validate against acceptance criteria and requirements.

People and conflict questions: choose the win-win path

Some questions test team dynamics and conflict management. In general, the exam prefers approaches that resolve the root issue and protect relationships (think: confronting/collaborating over avoiding). If a project is slipping, “hope it gets better” is never the answer—use a plan, communicate, and remove blockers.

Governance and procurement scenarios: treat constraints as real

Governance questions are often about constraints you can’t negotiate away: compliance, security requirements, procurement rules, and approval workflows. In these scenarios, the most correct answer is usually the one that aligns with policy and reduces risk—even if it slows delivery.

If you want to connect Project+ to longer-term career paths, you’ll often see it compared to more advanced project management credentials. Project+ is designed for IT professionals managing projects alongside other responsibilities, while credentials like PMP are typically for dedicated project managers with substantial documented experience.

A practical 2–3 week Project+ study sprint (WGU-friendly)

Most students who pass quickly don’t study endlessly—they study with structure. Your goal is to build scenario fluency and tool recognition without burning out. Here’s a straightforward sprint you can adapt based on your schedule.

To make this plan work as a busy adult, focus on daily outputs instead of daily hours. A good “output” is something you can reuse on exam day: a one-page notes sheet, a trap-answer list, a chart recognition checklist, or a set of corrected practice questions.

If your weekdays are tight, aim for 60–90 minutes per day and keep your weekends for deeper work:

  • Weekday session (60–90 minutes): 20 minutes concepts + 30 minutes practice + 10 minutes review notes.
  • Weekend session (2–4 hours): one full domain review + a timed mixed set + deep review of misses.

Week 1: Build the mental model (concepts + lifecycle)

  1. Day 1–2: skim the exam domains and build a one-page “cheat sheet” of documents, roles, and processes.
  2. Day 3–4: study methodologies (Waterfall, Agile, Hybrid) and when each fits.
  3. Day 5–7: deep focus on lifecycle phases: initiation → planning → execution → monitoring/controlling → closing.
Make it real: Pick a project you understand (a website launch, a cloud migration, a system upgrade) and map it through the lifecycle. If you can explain the “why” behind each artifact, you’re learning the exam.

Week 2: Tools, charts, and scenario reps

  1. Day 8–9: scheduling tools: Gantt, dependencies, critical path, float, and what changes do to the schedule.
  2. Day 10: documentation: WBS, work packages, change requests, risk register, communications plan.
  3. Day 11–12: Agile artifacts and ceremonies: backlog, sprint planning, standups, retrospectives, burndown/burnup.
  4. Day 13–14: practice questions by domain; review every miss and update your cheat sheet.

Week 3 (optional): Tighten weak areas and simulate the exam

If you have time, use the third week for two purposes: (1) fix your weak domain(s), and (2) train pacing so you don’t lose points to time pressure.

  • Take two timed practice sets.
  • Review missed topics and write short “if/then” rules (example: “CPI < 1 = over budget”).
  • Do a final mixed set and review your notes, not the whole book.

High-yield topics and common trap answers

Project+ questions can feel subjective. They’re not. There’s usually a “most correct” answer that matches the expected project management process. Here are the highest-yield areas to lock down.

Change control: know the sequence

Change control is one of the most tested pieces of scenario logic. The biggest trap is implementing a change before the impact is analyzed and approved.

Remember this order: Request → Impact analysis → Change control board (CCB) approval → Implementation → Validation.

When you see a question about a stakeholder request, ask yourself:

  • Do we have a formal change request?
  • Have we assessed impact on scope, schedule, budget, and risk?
  • Has the right authority approved it?

Agile and Hybrid: PK0-005 emphasizes modern delivery

PK0-005 increased the emphasis on Agile and Hybrid thinking. You don’t need to be a Scrum Master, but you do need to understand the purpose of ceremonies and artifacts, and how to handle change in iterative delivery.

  • Backlog basics: the backlog is prioritized work; refinement improves clarity and sequencing.
  • Burndown vs burnup: interpret what the chart indicates (work remaining vs scope growth + work done).
  • Retrospectives: improvement feedback loop, not a status meeting.

EVM (earned value): interpret more than calculate

Project+ sometimes includes earned value management concepts. The exam often cares more about what the number means than whether you can compute it fast.

  • CPI < 1.0: over budget.
  • SPI < 1.0: behind schedule.
Fast win: If you can interpret CPI/SPI and explain what corrective action is appropriate, you’re ahead of most test-takers.

Critical path and float: think in consequences

When a task on the critical path slips, the end date slips—unless you change the plan. When a non-critical task slips, float gets consumed first. Practice reading these scenarios until you can answer them without doing math.

Practice questions: how to use CertMaster and avoid burnout

Most WGU students use a combination of official materials and practice banks. A common experience is that CertMaster practice questions feel harder than the real exam—especially because they are wordy and the distractors can feel “almost correct.”

Instead of chasing a perfect score, use practice questions for two purposes:

  • Find your weak domain: if you’re missing governance or documentation questions, you need targeted reps.
  • Learn CompTIA-style wording: “best,” “first,” “next,” and “most appropriate” are telling you to follow the process.
Don’t just retake the same questions: If you recognize answers by memory, you’re not improving your real exam performance.

As a rough readiness signal, many students aim for consistent practice scores around 75–80% with thorough review. Your trend matters more than any single attempt.

PBQs: how to handle performance-based questions

PBQs can intimidate first-time test-takers, but Project+ PBQs are often simpler than expected. You may see tasks like:

  • Dragging and dropping project activities into the correct order.
  • Matching a document to a lifecycle phase.
  • Interpreting a chart or schedule scenario.

Use this PBQ approach:

  1. Read the scenario twice: once for context, once for the specific ask.
  2. Identify the category: process, tool, role/responsibility, or governance.
  3. Eliminate “do it now” answers: if analysis/approval is missing, implementation is usually wrong.
  4. Don’t overthink: PBQs are designed to test practical judgment, not trick you with edge cases.

Exam-day checklist (proctoring, pacing, and stress control)

Even if you know the content, test-day issues can cost you time and confidence. Treat your exam day like a small project: plan it, reduce risk, and execute.

Before you start

  • Clear your desk and workspace and keep only permitted items.
  • Make sure lighting is strong enough for the proctor to see you.
  • Have a valid ID ready before you connect.
  • Close apps and tabs you don’t need.

During the exam

  • Use the “flag and return” strategy: don’t burn 6 minutes on one question.
  • Watch for “best/first/next” language: it tells you what the exam is asking.
  • Trust your process answers: analyze, communicate, document, approve, then implement.

After the exam: what to do if you pass (or don’t)

If you pass, take a moment and document your win. Save the score report and note which areas felt hardest. Those insights help later when you take other certifications or manage project-heavy coursework.

If you don’t pass, don’t spiral. Your best next move is targeted remediation:

  • Identify the domain(s) where you were weakest.
  • Review the concepts behind each miss (don’t just memorize the correct option).
  • Do focused practice sets only in the weak areas until your score trend improves.
Acceleration tip: When you pass Project+, you gain a reusable “project thinking” framework that helps in other courses—especially planning-heavy assessments and capstone work.
