WGU C768 Technical Communication: PA Tips and Writing Guide

December 24, 2025 8 min read Course Help

WGU C768 Technical Communication builds the professional writing and presentation skills employers expect from IT graduates. If you are in the BSIT track, this is where you learn to deliver clear, audience-focused communication and polished technical artifacts. This guide summarizes the official course focus, then walks you through a practical writing workflow so you can finish your performance assessment tasks faster and with fewer revisions.

If you want a deeper overview of the program itself, see WGU BSIT: How to Complete Your IT Degree in Record Time.

Official course focus (WGU program guide)

The WGU BSIT program guide describes Technical Communication as a course that develops professional communication skills. It highlights editing professional communications, evaluating the impact of professional etiquette in digital environments, creating persuasive, informational, and research-based technical artifacts, and delivering multimedia presentations.

Skills you must demonstrate

Use these outcomes as your target checklist while you work:

  • Edit professional communications for clarity, tone, and accuracy
  • Evaluate how digital etiquette affects trust and outcomes
  • Create technical artifacts that are persuasive, informational, or research-driven
  • Deliver a multimedia presentation that communicates a clear message

Common artifacts and deliverables

Your exact tasks may vary by course version, but most C768 assessments include multiple artifacts such as memos, reports, instructions, or proposals, plus a presentation or slide deck. Approach each deliverable as a real workplace document with a clear audience and purpose.

Tip: Identify the audience on every task. A document written for a technical peer will look different from one written for executives or customers.

A writing workflow that actually works

Technical writing is faster when you use a repeatable workflow:

  1. Plan: Outline the purpose, audience, and required sections.
  2. Draft: Write quickly using headings and bullets before polishing.
  3. Revise: Fix structure, clarity, and tone before grammar cleanup.

Most students lose time by polishing too early. Save line edits for the final pass.

Structure for a strong technical document

Use a consistent structure so evaluators can follow your logic fast:

  • Title + purpose: One sentence that explains the goal
  • Audience assumptions: What they know and need
  • Main body: Headings, steps, or sections aligned to the task
  • Conclusion or next steps: Clear action or recommendation

Clear headings do more than formatting. They are proof that you understand the task and can guide the reader.

Professional tone and digital etiquette

C768 explicitly addresses etiquette in digital communication. In your writing, prioritize clarity, respect, and professionalism. Avoid slang, vague phrasing, or emotional language. When in doubt, choose a calm, concise sentence that states the action or decision.

Using evidence and sources

Persuasive and research-based artifacts require evidence. Use reputable sources, cite them consistently, and summarize them in your own words. The goal is not just to cite, but to show how the evidence supports your recommendation.

Visuals, formatting, and readability

Good technical communication is easy to scan. Use short paragraphs, numbered steps, and tables or visuals when they add clarity. Captions and labels help readers understand charts quickly.

Multimedia presentation tips

If your assessment includes a presentation, keep it structured and professional:

  • Use a clean slide template and limit text to key points
  • Speak to the audience, not the slide
  • End with a clear recommendation or summary

Rubric mapping checklist

Performance assessments are won by careful rubric alignment. Use this workflow:

  1. List every rubric requirement in a checklist.
  2. Map each requirement to a specific section or slide.
  3. Verify coverage before you submit.

For a deeper rubric strategy, see WGU Performance Assessments: Write Papers That Pass First Time.

Warning: The fastest way to get sent back for revisions is missing a rubric line item. If a requirement is not visible in your document, the evaluator cannot award credit for it.

Quality checks before submission

  • Every requirement has a visible section or bullet
  • Headings match the task language
  • Grammar and tone are professional
  • Sources are cited consistently
  • Visuals are labeled and easy to read

Study plan (2 to 3 weeks)

  1. Week 1: Review the task prompts, outline each artifact, and gather sources.
  2. Week 2: Draft and revise the written artifacts.
  3. Week 3: Final edits and complete the presentation deliverable.

If you only have one week

  1. Day 1: Build outlines and checklist for every requirement.
  2. Days 2-4: Draft and revise artifacts in order of complexity.
  3. Days 5-6: Create slides and presentation notes.
  4. Day 7: Run the quality checklist and submit.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Writing without a clear audience in mind
  • Skipping citations for research-based claims
  • Submitting a draft that is not aligned to the rubric
  • Using long paragraphs instead of scannable sections

Ready-to-submit checklist

  • I can explain the purpose and audience of every artifact
  • All rubric requirements are covered with headings or bullets
  • The writing is clear, concise, and professional
  • The presentation summarizes the key recommendation
