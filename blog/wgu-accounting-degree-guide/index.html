WGU Accounting Degree: Your Path to CPA and Beyond

December 19, 2025 12 min read Degree Guides

Thinking about a WGU accounting degree but wondering if it’s respected, worth it, and realistic to complete while working full-time? You’re asking the right questions.

This guide breaks down how WGU Accounting works, what you’ll learn, how to plan for the CPA path, and a step-by-step strategy to finish efficiently (without burning out).

Who this is for: Working adults who want an accredited accounting degree, are considering CPA eligibility, and want a faster/leaner study system.

WGU Accounting at a glance

WGU is a competency-based university. Instead of weekly discussion posts and timed semesters, you progress by proving mastery through assessments. That can be a huge advantage in accounting, where many topics build logically and repeat across courses.

  • Format: Online, self-paced within a term
  • Term length: 6 months (you can complete more courses if you move faster)
  • Assessments: Objective Assessments (OAs) + Performance Assessments (PAs) depending on the course
  • Best for: Self-motivated students who want flexibility and a clear finish line

Tuition, term structure, and total cost planning

WGU tuition is typically a flat rate per 6-month term. Your total cost depends on how many terms you take—so the biggest lever is your pace.

What you’ll learn (skills that matter in real accounting jobs)

Accounting careers reward fundamentals: financial statements, controls, analysis, and clear communication. A good program should build you toward professional-level competency, not just “passing tests.”

Expect to work through topics like:

  • Financial accounting: financial statements, revenue/expense recognition, adjusting entries
  • Managerial & cost accounting: budgeting, variance analysis, cost behavior
  • Tax & auditing basics: common frameworks and how to think through scenarios
  • Business core: economics, business law, operations, and decision-making
  • Tools: spreadsheets and data skills that show up everywhere

CPA planning (read this before you commit)

If the CPA is your goal, you need to plan backwards from your state board requirements. The biggest gotcha is education requirements—many states require 150 semester credit hours and a specific mix of accounting/business coursework.

Important: CPA eligibility is state-specific. Use NASBA and your state board to verify exactly what’s required (education hours, course categories, experience, and exam steps).

CPA eligibility checklist

Step What to do Why it matters
1) Confirm state rules Check NASBA + your state board requirements Avoid surprises about course types/credit totals
2) Map your degree plan Compare your program coursework to requirements Ensures your education matches eligibility categories
3) Plan the 150 hours If needed, plan additional credits (grad courses, extra undergrad credits, etc.) Many candidates need more than a standard bachelor’s
4) Plan experience Understand supervision/experience rules for your state Licensure isn’t just passing exams

Career paths: where a WGU accounting degree can take you

Accounting is one of the clearest “skill-to-job” pipelines. Common outcomes include:

  • Staff accountant / junior accountant (public or industry)
  • Accounts payable/receivable specialist (often a stepping stone)
  • Tax associate (public accounting track)
  • Audit associate (public accounting track)
  • Financial analyst / FP&A (with strong Excel + business skills)

For salary and outlook context, the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes data for accountants and auditors (use it as a baseline, not a promise).

How to finish faster (without cutting corners)

Acceleration is about removing wasted motion. The simplest strategy is to build a repeatable weekly system and keep momentum.

  1. Start with your transfer evaluation: bring in eligible credits before you enroll.
  2. Use an “OA first” strategy: take a pre-assessment early, then study only weak areas.
  3. Use a rubric-first strategy for PAs: outline to the rubric and submit when requirements are met.
  4. Protect deep work blocks: 2–3 focused sessions per week beats daily scattered studying.
  5. Schedule assessments early: deadlines create urgency and prevent drift.
