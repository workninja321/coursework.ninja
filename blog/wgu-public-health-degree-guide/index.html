WGU B.S. Public Health: A Guide to the New Leavitt School Program

January 20, 2026 9 min read Degree Guides

If you want a career where your work improves the health of entire communities—not just individual patients—the WGU public health bachelors is one of the newest options in the Leavitt School of Health. This fully online, competency-based program is built for working adults who need flexibility but still want a rigorous path into public health and community wellness careers.

In this guide, well walk through how the new online public health degree at WGU is structured, what it costs, how long it typically takes, and the kinds of jobs it can open up. Well also talk candidly about questions students are already asking on Reddit and elsewhereincluding CEPH accreditation, statistics-heavy courses, and how to survive the grind as a busy adult learner.

Program overview: WGU B.S. Public Health at a glance

The B.S. Public Health is part of WGUs Leavitt School of Health, alongside degrees like Health and Human Services, Health Science, Psychology, and Health Information Management. The curriculum is designed to introduce you to the foundations of population health while building the analytic, communication, and policy awareness skills you need to contribute to real-world public health initiatives.

Instead of seat-time or weekly participation grades, WGU uses a competency-based model. Each course is passed by completing performance assessments (projects, papers) and objective assessments (OAs). If you already understand the materialfrom prior coursework or professional experienceyou can accelerate quickly; if a topic is new, you can slow down without worrying about falling behind a fixed lecture schedule.

  • School: Leavitt School of Health (undergraduate)
  • Format: 100% online, asynchronous, competency-based
  • Courses: ~33 total, mixing general education and public health core
  • Focus areas: Foundations of public health, epidemiology, mental health, chronic and infectious disease, health promotion, health communication, and equity
  • Career focus: Entry-level public health roles and preparation for graduate study (e.g., MPH)

If youre still deciding between public health and more administrative paths, you may also want to compare this program to business-focused options like WGUs healthcare administration degree (see our WGU Healthcare Management guide) to clarify which path better fits your long-term goals.

Cost, tuition, and how long the degree takes

One of the biggest reasons students choose WGU is cost transparency. For the BS public health WGU program, tuition is currently listed in WGUs tuition tables at $4,210 per six-month term plus a $200 E-Books and Resources fee. That flat rate covers every course you complete in that termwhether you finish three courses or ten.

Because pricing is per term, not per credit, the total cost of your degree depends heavily on your pace. WGU reports that around 63% of Leavitt School undergraduates in similar programs finish in about 24 months. Faster students who transfer in credit or push hard each term can potentially finish in as little as 18 months; others, especially those with heavy work or family demands, may need three to four years.

Acceleration tip: Before your first term, map out which courses you might be able to accelerate based on prior experience (for example, introductory health or communication courses). Clearing those early can free up time for tougher classes later like epidemiology or research methods.

Remember that tuition numbers change periodically. Always verify the most current Leavitt School tuition page before you enroll, and factor in any employer tuition assistance, scholarships, or federal aid you may be eligible for.

What youll study: Courses and skills in WGU public health

While WGU can tweak course lineups over time, public materials and early student reports point to a curriculum built around three big buckets: general education, public health foundations, and advanced application courses. Across roughly 33 courses, you can expect a blend of writing, data analysis, and applied projects.

Foundations of public health

Early in the program youll complete courses like Foundations of Public Health and Community and Public Health. These cover the history of public health, core functions of public health agencies, health disparities, social determinants of health, and how public health differs from traditional clinical care. Students on Reddit note that these courses are reading-heavy but manageable with consistent daily study.

Epidemiology, data, and research skills

As you move deeper into the degree, youll hit courses in epidemiology and research methods. These build skills in reading and interpreting public health studies, working with basic statistics, and understanding measures like incidence, prevalence, and risk ratios. Many students are nervous about the math, but WGU provides course instructors, cohorts, and plenty of supplemental resources to help.

If statistics has always been a sticking point for you, consider budgeting extra time in your study plan for these courses and using external resources (YouTube explainer videos, practice problem books) on top of WGUs learning materials.

Mental health, chronic disease, and health promotion

Public health is as much about behavior and environment as it is about pathogens. Expect coursework in mental health, chronic and infectious diseases, health promotion, and health communication. Youll explore how lifestyle, environment, and systemic inequities drive outcomes, and how to design interventions that are culturally sensitive and realistic for the communities you serve.

These courses often culminate in performance assessments where you design educational materials, outreach campaigns, or program proposals. The skills you build here translate directly into roles like health education specialist or community outreach coordinator.

Admissions, prerequisites, and who this program is for

Admissions for Leavitt School programs layer on top of WGUs general undergraduate admissions. Exact requirements can change, so always check the official admissions page, but you can expect to provide prior transcripts and demonstrate readiness for college-level work.

From a fit perspective, the WGU public health degree tends to work best for:

  • Working adults already in healthcare-adjacent roles (medical assistants, community outreach staff, case managers) who want a formal public health credential.
  • Career changers coming from education, social services, or nonprofit work who want to move into population health and prevention.
  • Students planning a long-term path toward an MPH or other graduate program, but who need a flexible online bachelors first.

If your interests are more clinical (bedside nursing, advanced practice) you may be better served by a nursing pathwaysee our WGU nursing program guide. If you care most about hospital operations, budgets, and staffing, healthcare administration or health information management could be a better fit.

Careers and outcomes for WGU public health graduates

A bachelors in public health is typically an entry point into a wide range of population-focused roles. WGU positions this degree as preparation for jobs such as community health worker, health education specialist, program coordinator, and outreach specialist in settings like local health departments, nonprofits, community clinics, and health systems.

National data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics shows that health education specialists had a median annual wage of about $63,000 in 2024, with employment projected to grow around 4% from 2024 to 2034about as fast as average. Actual salaries depend heavily on location, employer type, experience, and whether you move into supervisory or specialist roles.

WGU reports that Leavitt School undergraduates see an average salary increase of roughly $11,000 after completing their programs. While individual results vary, the combination of a recognized bachelors degree, experience from your current role, and the ability to talk fluently about epidemiology, health promotion, and data can make you competitive for roles that previously required 1any bachelors in a health field.

If youre focused on maximizing ROI, consider how this program fits into your broader plan: which roles are realistic right after graduation, which may require an MPH or certifications, and how long you want to stay on the front lines vs. moving into program leadership.

Accreditation, CEPH, and choosing the right path

A recurring topic in student discussions is whether WGUs public health programs are accredited by CEPH (Council on Education for Public Health). At the time of writing, publicly available information and Reddit threads suggest that the B.S. Public Health is not CEPH-accredited. That doesnt automatically make the degree a bad choice, but it does mean you should think carefully about your long-term plans.

Some employers and graduate programs, especially government agencies and certain MPH programs, explicitly prefer or require degrees from CEPH-accredited institutions. Others are more flexible and will consider any regionally accredited bachelors, especially when paired with strong experience. The safest approach is to:

  • Confirm WGUs current accreditation status directly with an enrollment counselor.
  • Check admissions requirements for any target MPH programs youre considering.
  • Look at real job postings in your state to see how often they mention CEPH.

Important: If your dream role or graduate program explicitly requires a CEPH-accredited bachelors, that may be a signal to look at other schools. If requirements are more flexible, WGUs affordability and pace can still make it a strong option.

What students are saying so far

Because the program is new, there arent yet years of detailed reviews. However, early posts on r/WGU and related communities highlight some consistent themes:

  • Students are excited about the focus on community impact and the ability to stay in their current jobs while earning a degree.
  • Foundational courses like Foundations of Public Health are described as manageable but reading-intensive.
  • Many learners worry about statistics-heavy courses and ask for advice on tackling epidemiology and research methods.
  • Working adults emphasize the need for disciplined daily study habits to avoid getting overwhelmed at the end of the term.
  • CEPH accreditation questions come up frequently, especially from students considering an eventual MPH.

The bottom line: success in this program has less to do with your current math skills and more to do with your willingness to build consistent routines, ask for help early, and treat each term like a project with milestones.

Planning your terms and avoiding burnout

The biggest hidden challenge in a competency-based program is not the contentits pacing. When there are no weekly lectures or mandatory discussion boards, its easy to underestimate how much effort a six-month term actually requires. Here are strategies we see successful WGU students use across health programs:

Work backward from your target graduation date

Start by deciding when you reasonably want to finish: two years, two and a half, three? Then divide the ~33 courses across that timeline. A two-year plan, for example, means averaging about four to five courses per term. Looking at that number early helps you decide whether you need to request lighter work hours, line up childcare, or adjust your expectations.

Front-load familiar courses, protect time for harder ones

Many WGU students work full-time. A smart strategy is to clear general education or familiar health courses early in each term, then protect big uninterrupted blocks of time for more demanding classes like epidemiology or capstone-style projects. That pattern keeps your momentum up and reduces last-minute panic.

Use support aggressively

Course instructors, program mentors, and peer communities exist for a reason. The students who thrive are the ones who raise their hand early, book 1:1s before they are in crisis, and arent shy about asking 1dumb questions. Treat WGU like a toolkit; you dont have to figure everything out alone.
