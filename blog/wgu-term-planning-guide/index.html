WGU Term Planning Guide: How to Structure Your 6-Month Term for Success

December 17, 2025 12 min read Study Tips

WGU's 6-month term structure is both a blessing and a challenge. Unlike traditional semesters, you have complete flexibility to work at your own pace—but this freedom requires strategic planning to maximize your success and avoid common pitfalls.

This guide will show you how to structure your term for maximum efficiency, sequence courses strategically, work effectively with your Program Mentor, and adjust your plan when life happens.

Understanding WGU's Term Structure

Before diving into planning strategies, it's important to understand how WGU's terms work:

The Basics

  • Duration: Each term lasts exactly 6 months (182-183 days)
  • Flat tuition: You pay one price regardless of how many courses you complete
  • Minimum requirement: 12 competency units (CUs) to maintain satisfactory progress
  • No maximum: Complete as many courses as you can demonstrate mastery in
  • Term break: Short break between terms (typically 7-14 days)

How Terms Differ from Traditional Semesters

  • No scheduled class meetings or fixed deadlines (except final day of term)
  • Start courses whenever you're ready, not at set dates
  • Take assessments as soon as you're prepared
  • Add courses mid-term if you complete your initial plan early
Pro Tip: Your term start date is locked when you enroll. Plan your enrollment timing carefully—starting at the beginning of a month gives you a clean calendar to work with.

Setting Realistic Course Goals

The most common term planning mistake is either overcommitting (leading to stress and burnout) or undercommitting (wasting tuition dollars and time).

Calculate Your Available Time

Start with an honest assessment of your weekly study availability:

  1. Work hours: Factor in your job, commute, and overtime
  2. Family commitments: Childcare, household responsibilities, family time
  3. Sleep and self-care: Don't sacrifice health for speed
  4. Social/personal time: Maintain balance to avoid burnout

Realistic weekly study time estimates:

  • Light pace (15-20 hours/week): 12-18 CUs per term (minimum to moderate)
  • Moderate pace (20-30 hours/week): 18-30 CUs per term
  • Aggressive pace (30-40+ hours/week): 30-50+ CUs per term

Match Goals to Your Situation

Consider these factors when setting your term goals:

  • Prior knowledge: Courses in your field of expertise move faster
  • Course difficulty: Some courses are notoriously harder than others
  • Assessment types: OA courses typically complete faster than PA courses
  • Life circumstances: Known busy periods (tax season, moving, etc.)

First Term vs. Subsequent Terms

Your approach to term planning should evolve as you gain experience with WGU's model.

First Term Strategy: Conservative and Learning

Your first term is about finding your rhythm. Plan conservatively:

  • Start with minimum requirement: Plan for 12-15 CUs initially
  • Choose easier courses first: Build confidence with subjects you know
  • Learn the system: Understand how pre-assessments, OAs, and PAs work
  • Test your pace: See how quickly you actually complete courses
  • Build study habits: Establish routines that work with your schedule
Pro Tip: If you complete your planned courses before month 4 of your first term, contact your Program Mentor to add more courses. You can always add, but you can't remove courses mid-term.

Subsequent Terms: Optimize and Accelerate

After your first term, you'll have data on your actual pace. Use it to plan more effectively:

  • Review your velocity: How many CUs did you actually complete?
  • Identify patterns: Which course types were faster/slower for you?
  • Plan aggressively (with buffer): Load your term plan but leave room for life
  • Front-load difficulty: Tackle hard courses early when motivation is high
  • Consider acceleration: If you're excelling, push toward faster completion

How to Sequence Courses Strategically

The order you take courses matters enormously for maintaining momentum and avoiding burnout.

OA vs PA Sequencing

Mix objective assessments (exams) and performance assessments (papers) strategically:

  • Start with OAs: Build momentum with faster completions and immediate feedback
  • Alternate types: While waiting for PA grading, work on OA courses
  • Group PAs mid-term: Schedule 2-3 PAs together when you have dedicated writing time
  • End with OAs: Finish strong with courses that provide instant results

Easy vs. Hard Course Sequencing

Balance difficulty throughout your term:

  1. Weeks 1-8: Mix of easy-medium courses to build momentum
  2. Weeks 9-16: Tackle 1-2 difficult courses while energy is still high
  3. Weeks 17-20: Medium difficulty—maintain steady progress
  4. Weeks 21-26: Easier courses as a buffer for unexpected challenges
Warning: Don't save all your hardest courses for the end of your term. If you hit a wall, you won't have time to recover.

Related Course Batching

Group similar courses together when possible:

  • Take multiple finance courses in sequence while concepts are fresh
  • Batch all IT certification courses together
  • Complete related English/writing courses consecutively
  • Study mathematical subjects when you're in "math mode"

Prerequisites First

Some courses must be taken in order. Work with your mentor to identify:

  • Official prerequisites (system-enforced)
  • Recommended sequences (mentor-suggested)
  • Courses that build on each other conceptually

Working with Your Mentor on Term Planning

Your Program Mentor is your partner in creating an effective term plan. Here's how to make the most of this relationship:

Before Your Term Starts

  • Discuss your goals: Be clear about your desired completion timeline
  • Share your schedule: Inform them about work, family, and other commitments
  • Ask about course difficulty: Get insider knowledge on which courses are hardest
  • Understand prerequisites: Know which courses must be taken in order
  • Review assessment types: Balance OAs and PAs appropriately

During Your Term

  • Provide progress updates: Check in regularly, not just during scheduled calls
  • Request course additions: Don't wait if you're moving faster than planned
  • Communicate challenges early: If you're struggling, tell them immediately
  • Ask for exam unlocks: When you're ready, request assessments be unlocked
  • Discuss sequence changes: If a course isn't working, pivot to another
Pro Tip: Schedule your weekly mentor calls at consistent times. This creates accountability and regular checkpoints for your progress. For more tips, see our WGU Mentor Tips guide.

What to Ask Your Mentor

Get the most value from mentor conversations with these questions:

  • "Which courses in my program are known to be easier/harder?"
  • "What's a realistic timeline for completing [specific course]?"
  • "Should I take Course A before Course B, or does it matter?"
  • "Can you recommend resources beyond the official course materials?"
  • "What pace would you suggest for someone in my situation?"

Adjusting Your Plan Mid-Term

No term plan survives contact with reality. Here's how to adjust when things change:

When You're Ahead of Schedule

  • Add courses immediately: Contact your mentor to add more courses to your term
  • Take pre-assessments: Test your readiness for upcoming courses
  • Front-load next term: Get a head start on difficult courses
  • Maintain momentum: Don't slow down—use the motivation while you have it

When You're Behind Schedule

  • Don't panic: Falling behind initial projections is normal
  • Reassess priorities: Focus on completing the minimum 12 CUs first
  • Identify obstacles: What's slowing you down? Course difficulty? Life events?
  • Adjust expectations: It's okay to move courses to next term
  • Get help: Reach out to course instructors, mentors, or tutors

When to Pivot to a Different Course

Sometimes the best move is to temporarily switch to another course:

  • You're truly stuck and additional study isn't helping
  • The course is taking much longer than expected
  • You need a mental break from difficult material
  • Prerequisites weren't as solid as you thought
Important: Discuss any major plan changes with your mentor first. They can help you strategize and ensure you're still on track for the term minimum.

Planning Around Life Events

Life doesn't pause for your degree. Build flexibility into your term plan for known and unknown events.

Scheduled Events (Holidays, Vacations, Work Deadlines)

  • Identify busy periods upfront: Mark them on your term calendar immediately
  • Build buffer time: Plan lighter course loads during these weeks
  • Front-load the term: Complete more courses early before busy periods hit
  • Schedule PAs strategically: Don't submit papers right before vacation (grading delays)

Common Busy Periods to Plan For

  • Holidays: Thanksgiving, December holidays, family obligations
  • Work cycles: Tax season (if in accounting), fiscal year-end, busy seasons
  • School schedules: If you have kids, plan around their breaks and testing periods
  • Personal events: Weddings, moves, major purchases (home buying), medical procedures

Unexpected Disruptions

Build resilience into your plan for unplanned events:

  • Keep a 2-week buffer: Don't plan to use every single day of your term
  • Have "easy" courses in reserve: Quick wins if you need to make up time
  • Know your options: Term extensions are possible in extreme circumstances
  • Communicate immediately: Tell your mentor if something major happens

Common Term Planning Mistakes to Avoid

Learn from others' mistakes and avoid these common term planning pitfalls:

1. Overplanning Your First Term

New students often plan for 30+ CUs in their first term, then burn out by month 2. Start conservative and add courses if you're exceeding expectations.

2. All PAs at the End

Waiting until the final weeks to tackle all your writing assignments creates unnecessary stress and risks grading delays that could push you past your term end date.

3. Not Using Pre-Assessments

Students waste time studying material they already know. Always take the pre-assessment first to identify exactly what you need to study.

4. Ignoring Course Reputations

Some courses are notoriously difficult. Research course difficulty (Reddit r/WGU, Facebook groups) before planning them back-to-back with other hard courses.

5. Not Building in Buffer Time

Planning to use all 183 days of your term leaves no room for error. Always keep 1-2 weeks as a buffer for unexpected delays.

6. Working in Isolation

Students who don't engage with their mentor, course instructors, or study communities struggle more and take longer. Use the support systems available.

7. Comparing Your Pace to Others

Social media shows students completing degrees in 6 months, but these are outliers. Your pace should match your circumstances, not someone else's highlight reel.

8. Not Adjusting After First Course

Your first course completion gives you valuable data about your actual pace. Use it to adjust your plan rather than sticking to an unrealistic original timeline.
