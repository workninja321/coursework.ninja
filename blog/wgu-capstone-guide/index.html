WGU Capstone Project: Step-by-Step Guide to Completing Your Final Project

December 17, 2025 12 min read Exam Preparation

The capstone is the final hurdle between you and your WGU degree. It's designed to demonstrate everything you've learned by applying your knowledge to a real-world problem. While it may seem overwhelming, thousands of students successfully complete their capstones every year.

This guide walks you through the entire process, from topic selection to final submission, with proven strategies to pass on your first attempt.

What Is the WGU Capstone?

The WGU capstone is a comprehensive project that demonstrates your cumulative learning. Unlike regular courses, the capstone requires you to:

  • Identify a real problem in your field (business, IT, healthcare, education)
  • Propose a solution using knowledge from your degree program
  • Conduct research with literature review and methodology
  • Present findings in a written paper and narrated presentation

Think of it as a several-month undertaking that proves you can apply academic concepts to solve real-world problems.

Capstone Requirements

All WGU capstones share common components, though specifics vary by program:

  • Research Proposal - Define your problem, solution, and methodology
  • Site Permission - Get approval to conduct research at an organization
  • Literature Review - Analyze existing research on your topic
  • Data Collection - Gather evidence using approved instruments
  • Results & Conclusions - Present findings and recommendations
  • Final Written Paper - Comprehensive document (typically 30-50 pages)
  • Narrated Presentation - Video summary of your project
Important: Read ALL task requirements before starting. WGU provides detailed instructions and videos for each capstone task. Many students fail because they skip these resources.

Choosing Your Topic

Topic selection is the most critical decision. A good topic makes the capstone manageable; a poor topic can derail your entire project.

Best Practices for Topic Selection

  1. Use a workplace problem: Past or present work projects make excellent capstones. You have access to data, context, and stakeholders.
  2. Propose a technical solution: Avoid topics that only deliver information. Your capstone should solve a specific problem.
  3. Choose something you understand: Don't pick a topic just because it sounds impressive. Pick one where you can demonstrate competency.
  4. Consider scope carefully: Too broad and you'll never finish. Too narrow and you won't have enough content.
IT Capstone Tip: Your capstone can be very similar to your C850 Emerging Technologies project. The difference is you get to choose the client and problem to solve.

Topics to Avoid

  • Topics without measurable outcomes
  • Problems you can't actually solve
  • Subjects requiring data you can't access
  • Overly complex implementations

Step-by-Step Process

1 Watch All Instructional Videos

Start by watching the provided videos: Project Idea Overview, Task 2: Proposal Overview, and any program-specific guidance. These explain exactly what evaluators expect.

2 Submit Your Topic for Approval

Before writing your full proposal, get your topic approved. Your instructor mentor will tell you if the topic is viable or needs adjustment.

3 Write Your Proposal (Task 2)

The proposal defines your problem, solution, methodology, and timeline. It's your roadmap. Get this approved before proceeding.

4 Conduct Literature Review

Research existing studies on your topic. Use WGU Library resources. Aim for 15-25 scholarly sources depending on your program.

5 Collect and Analyze Data

Implement your data collection method. This might be surveys, interviews, system analysis, or other approved instruments.

6 Write Final Paper (Task 3)

Compile everything into your final document. Follow the rubric exactly. Address every requirement explicitly.

7 Create Narrated Presentation

Record a video presentation summarizing your project. Most programs require 10-15 minutes. Practice before recording.

Strategies for Success

Use the Capstone Archive

WGU provides a Model Capstone Archive with exemplary student projects. Study these to understand structure, formatting, and depth expected. The archive also showcases Capstone Excellence Award winners.

Follow the Rubric Exactly

Your capstone is evaluated against a specific rubric. Address every criterion explicitly. Use the same language from the rubric in your paper headers when possible.

Use WGU Writing Center

The Writing Center offers live Q&A sessions and can review drafts. While they can't guarantee you'll pass, they can help with APA formatting, professional communication, and source integration.

Stick to Your Timeline

Many students create a timeline in their proposal then ignore it. Set milestones and hold yourself accountable. Check in with your mentor regularly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting without reading requirements: The #1 reason for returns. Read everything first.
  • Poor topic selection: Choosing topics that are too broad, too narrow, or unsolvable.
  • Ignoring the timeline: The capstone takes months. Procrastination is your enemy.
  • Skipping the mentor: Your instructor mentor is there to help. Use them.
  • Copying examples verbatim: Use archive examples as guides, not templates to copy.
  • Weak literature review: Use scholarly sources. Wikipedia doesn't count.
  • Incomplete rubric coverage: Every rubric item must be addressed explicitly.

What If Your Capstone Gets Returned?

Don't panic. Most returned capstones need minor revisions. Here's what to do:

  1. Read feedback carefully: Evaluators explain exactly what's missing or needs improvement.
  2. Address each point: Go through feedback systematically. Don't assume you know what they want.
  3. Use the Writing Center: Get help revising before resubmitting.
  4. Talk to your mentor: They can clarify confusing feedback.
  5. Resubmit promptly: Don't let it sit. Address issues and resubmit within a week.
Encouragement: Even returned capstones are close to completion. You're not starting over. Most students pass on the second submission with targeted revisions.
