WGU C959 Discrete Mathematics I: How to Pass This Challenging Course

January 2, 2026 11 min read Course Help

WGU C959 Discrete Mathematics I is the gateway to mathematical thinking in computer science. This course challenges you to move beyond computational math into the world of logical reasoning, formal proofs, and abstract structures that underpin everything from algorithms to database design.

If you are pursuing the WGU Computer Science degree or any IT program requiring mathematical foundations, C959 is a critical milestone. This guide will help you understand what the course covers, how to approach each topic, and strategies to pass the OA efficiently.

What C959 Discrete Mathematics I Covers

C959 introduces the mathematical foundations that computer scientists use daily. The course is typically organized into several major units:

  • Propositional Logic: Truth tables, logical operators (AND, OR, NOT, implications), logical equivalences, and compound propositions
  • Predicate Logic: Quantifiers (for all, there exists), translating statements to logical notation, nested quantifiers
  • Proof Techniques: Direct proofs, proof by contrapositive, proof by contradiction, proof by cases
  • Sets and Set Operations: Set notation, subsets, unions, intersections, complements, Cartesian products, power sets
  • Relations: Binary relations, properties (reflexive, symmetric, transitive), equivalence relations, partial orders
  • Functions: Injective, surjective, bijective functions, composition, inverse functions
Tip: The course builds sequentially. Logic and proof techniques appear in every subsequent topic. Master them early, and the rest becomes significantly easier.

Mastering Propositional and Predicate Logic

Logic forms the language of discrete mathematics. Before you can prove anything, you need to express statements precisely and manipulate them correctly.

Key Logical Operators to Know

  • Conjunction (AND): p AND q is true only when both p and q are true
  • Disjunction (OR): p OR q is true when at least one of p or q is true
  • Negation (NOT): Reverses the truth value
  • Implication (IF-THEN): p implies q is false only when p is true and q is false
  • Biconditional (IF AND ONLY IF): True when both sides have the same truth value

Practice building truth tables until you can do them automatically. Know the key equivalences: De Morgan's laws, contrapositive, and distribution.

Common Mistake: Many students confuse implication with biconditional. Remember: "if p then q" does NOT mean "if q then p." The contrapositive (if not q then not p) is equivalent, but the converse is not.

Proof Techniques That Appear on the OA

Proofs are where most students struggle, but they follow predictable patterns. Once you recognize the structure, you can apply it systematically.

Direct Proof

Assume the hypothesis is true, then use logical steps to show the conclusion must also be true. This works well for straightforward implications.

Proof by Contrapositive

Instead of proving "if p then q," prove "if not q then not p." These are logically equivalent, and sometimes the contrapositive is easier to work with.

Proof by Contradiction

Assume the statement you want to prove is false, then show this leads to a logical contradiction. Therefore, the original statement must be true.

Proof by Cases

When a statement has multiple scenarios, prove each case separately. Cover all possibilities, and the proof is complete.

Pro Tip: When stuck on a proof, ask yourself: "What type of statement am I trying to prove?" Implications often yield to direct proof or contrapositive. Existence statements need examples. Universal statements need to work for all cases.

Sets, Relations, and Functions

These topics extend logic into structures you will use throughout computer science. Sets model collections, relations model connections, and functions model transformations.

Set Operations to Master

  • Union: Elements in A or B (or both)
  • Intersection: Elements in both A and B
  • Difference: Elements in A but not in B
  • Complement: Elements not in A (relative to a universal set)
  • Cartesian Product: All ordered pairs (a, b) where a is in A and b is in B
  • Power Set: The set of all subsets of A

Relation Properties

A relation R on a set A has specific properties that determine its behavior:

  • Reflexive: Every element relates to itself (aRa for all a)
  • Symmetric: If aRb then bRa
  • Transitive: If aRb and bRc then aRc
  • Equivalence Relation: Reflexive, symmetric, AND transitive

Function Types

  • Injective (One-to-One): Different inputs always give different outputs
  • Surjective (Onto): Every element in the codomain has at least one preimage
  • Bijective: Both injective and surjective (one-to-one correspondence)

OA Study Strategy

The C959 Objective Assessment tests your ability to apply concepts, not just recall definitions. Here is a proven approach:

  1. Take the Pre-Assessment First: Even before studying, take the PA to identify your baseline and weak areas
  2. Focus on Logic and Proofs: These appear in nearly every question type, directly or indirectly
  3. Practice by Hand: Write out truth tables, set operations, and proofs on paper, not just mentally
  4. Use External Resources: YouTube channels like Kimberly Brehm and TrevTutor explain discrete math concepts clearly
  5. Review the Coaching Report: After the PA, focus your study time on the competencies where you scored lowest

2-3 Week Study Plan

If you have some math background, this timeline is realistic. Adjust based on your pre-assessment results.

  • Days 1-4: Logic fundamentals - truth tables, logical equivalences, quantifiers
  • Days 5-8: Proof techniques - practice direct proofs, contrapositive, contradiction
  • Days 9-12: Sets and set operations - notation, operations, Venn diagrams, power sets
  • Days 13-16: Relations - properties, equivalence relations, partial orders
  • Days 17-19: Functions - types, composition, inverses
  • Days 20-21: Review weak areas, take practice assessments, schedule OA
Accelerator Tip: If you score above 70% on the pre-assessment, you may be ready to test within a week. Focus only on your weak competencies.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing implication with biconditional (they are NOT the same)
  • Forgetting that the empty set is a subset of every set
  • Mixing up reflexive, symmetric, and transitive properties
  • Not checking all cases in proof by cases
  • Assuming the converse is equivalent to the original implication
  • Skipping the pre-assessment and wasting time on topics you already know

Recommended Study Resources

  • Course Materials: The Zybooks textbook is comprehensive, work through the practice problems
  • YouTube: Kimberly Brehm's Discrete Math playlist covers all C959 topics
  • YouTube: TrevTutor has excellent explanations of proof techniques
  • Practice: Work through the unit reviews and cumulative reviews in the course

After completing C959, you will be prepared for WGU C949 Data Structures and Algorithms, where these mathematical foundations become practical tools for analyzing code.

Frequently Asked Questions

C959 covers propositional and predicate logic, sets and set operations, proof techniques (direct, contrapositive, contradiction), relations and their properties, functions, and introductory combinatorics. The course builds foundational mathematical reasoning skills used throughout computer science.

Difficulty varies by math background. Students comfortable with algebraic thinking and logical reasoning typically find it manageable. Those new to proof-based mathematics may need extra practice. Most students complete C959 in 2-4 weeks with focused study.

Start with the pre-assessment to identify weak areas. Focus on understanding logical operators, set notation, and proof structures. Practice writing proofs by hand. Use the course materials and supplement with YouTube videos from channels like Kimberly Brehm or TrevTutor.

You should memorize key logical equivalences, set identities, and proof templates. Focus on understanding when and how to apply them rather than rote memorization. The OA tests application more than recall.

C959 provides the mathematical foundation for data structures (C949), algorithms, and theoretical computer science courses. The logical reasoning and proof skills transfer directly to analyzing algorithm correctness and complexity.
