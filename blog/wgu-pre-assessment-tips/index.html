WGU Pre-Assessment Guide: Use PAs to Pass OAs Faster

January 15, 2025 8 min read Study Tips

WGU's pre-assessments are the most underutilized tool for accelerating your degree. Most students treat them as practice tests. Smart students use them as diagnostic tools that reveal exactly what to study—and what to skip.

This guide shows you how to strategically use pre-assessments to cut your study time dramatically while still passing OAs on the first attempt.

Key Insight: A pre-assessment isn't just practice—it's intelligence gathering. It shows you the exact competencies being tested and where your knowledge gaps are. Study the gaps, skip the rest.

What Are WGU Pre-Assessments?

Pre-assessments (PAs) are unproctored practice exams you can take from anywhere. Key characteristics:

  • Format: Same structure as the real OA (multiple choice, multi-select, etc.)
  • Content: Covers the same competencies but different questions
  • Time: Usually same time limit as the OA (or unlimited, depending on course)
  • Scoring: Competency-based feedback showing your performance by topic
  • Attempts: Unlimited—you can retake as many times as you want
  • Stakes: Zero—doesn't affect your grade or transcript

The "Pre-Assessment First" Strategy

This is the secret weapon of students who accelerate their degrees:

  1. Take the PA immediately when you start a new course—before reading any material. This reveals what you already know from prior experience or education.
  2. Analyze your results by competency area. The coaching report shows exactly which topics you passed and which you didn't.
  3. Study ONLY the weak areas. If you scored Competent in 4 out of 6 areas, you only need to focus on those 2 remaining areas.
  4. Take the PA again after focused studying to confirm you've filled the gaps.
  5. Schedule the OA when you're scoring Competent across all areas.
Time Savings Example: If a course has 6 competencies and you already know 4 of them, you've just eliminated 67% of your study time. What might take 3 weeks could take 1 week.

Interpreting Your PA Scores

WGU pre-assessments give you competency-level feedback. Here's what each score means:

Score Level What It Means Action Required
Exemplary Well above passing - mastery demonstrated Skip this section entirely. Move on.
Competent At or above passing level Light review only. Don't spend time here.
Approaching Close but not quite passing Targeted study needed. Focus 1-2 days here.
Not Competent Significant gap in knowledge Deep study required. This is your priority.

Reading the Coaching Report

After each PA, you get a coaching report. Here's how to use it effectively:

  • Look at competency breakdown - This shows which course objectives you've mastered vs. need work
  • Note the question distribution - Some competencies have more questions (weighted heavier)
  • Identify patterns - Are you missing conceptual questions? Application questions? Definition questions?
  • Map to course material - Each competency maps to specific chapters/modules in the course

Common Pre-Assessment Mistakes

Mistake #1: Over-preparing

Many students spend weeks studying before taking the PA. This wastes time on content you may already know. Take the PA first, then study strategically.

Mistake #2: Memorizing PA questions

The OA has different questions covering the same competencies. Memorizing PA answers without understanding concepts leads to OA failure. Focus on learning the underlying material.

Mistake #3: Ignoring "Approaching Competent"

Students often assume Approaching Competent is "close enough." It's not. These areas frequently cause OA failures. Treat them as seriously as Not Competent scores.

Mistake #4: Taking too many PAs

After 3-4 attempts, you start recognizing questions and get inflated scores. Limit yourself to 2-3 PA attempts maximum. If you need more, you need to study, not test.

Targeted Study Strategies by Competency Type

Different types of competencies require different study approaches:

Definition/Terminology

Use flashcards (Quizlet). Create them from course material. 15-20 minutes of drilling usually enough for these.

Concept Application

Work through examples in the course material. Don't just read—actually solve problems. Watch supplemental videos if available.

Process/Procedure

Create step-by-step checklists. Practice walking through scenarios. These are common in project management and IT process courses.

Analysis/Scenario

Focus on understanding "why" not just "what." Practice with case studies. These require deeper comprehension than memorization.

When to Schedule Your OA

Use this decision framework after taking your pre-assessment:

PA Result Recommendation Timeline to OA
Competent/Exemplary in ALL areas Schedule OA immediately 1-2 days (schedule now)
1-2 areas Approaching Competent Quick targeted review, retake PA 3-5 days
Multiple Approaching Competent Focused study on weak areas 1-2 weeks
Any Not Competent areas Deep study required 2-3 weeks minimum
Mostly Not Competent Full course study needed 3-4 weeks

Pre-Assessment vs OA: Key Differences

Understanding the differences helps set proper expectations:

Question Pool

PAs and OAs draw from the same competencies but different question banks. You won't see identical questions, but the topics and difficulty level are similar.

Environment

PAs are unproctored—take them anywhere, use any resources. OAs are proctored through Meazure Learning with strict requirements. The pressure difference can affect performance.

Difficulty Perception

Students often report the OA feeling harder. This is usually due to:

  • Proctoring stress
  • Unfamiliar question wording
  • No access to notes (unlike some PA attempts)
  • Time pressure feeling more real
Pro Tip: Take at least one PA attempt under OA conditions—timed, no notes, no breaks, no distractions. This prepares you for the actual test environment.

Course-Specific PA Tips

IT Certification Courses

For courses tied to certifications (CompTIA, AWS, etc.), the PA often mirrors certification exam format. See our certification guide for exam-specific strategies. These PAs are particularly accurate predictors.

Business Courses

Business courses often have scenario-based questions. The PA will show if you understand concepts in theory vs. application. Focus on case study practice for weak areas.

Math/Technical Courses

For courses like those in the Computer Science or BSIT programs, PAs test both computational skills and conceptual understanding. If you're passing calculations but failing theory questions (or vice versa), adjust your study accordingly.

Writing-Heavy Courses

Some courses combine PA (for knowledge) with Performance Assessment (for writing). Don't neglect the PA just because there's a paper component—both matter.

Using PAs to Accelerate

Pre-assessments are essential for degree acceleration. Here's how accelerators use them:

  1. Batch test at term start - Take PAs for multiple courses to identify quick wins
  2. Prioritize high-scoring courses - Knock out courses where you already score Competent
  3. Parallel track - While studying for one course, take PAs for the next to plan ahead
  4. Work with your mentor - Show strong PA scores to get approval for additional courses

The "Test Out" Workflow

  1. Start new course, immediately take PA
  2. Score Competent across all areas? Schedule OA for next day
  3. Pass OA, start next course same week
  4. Repeat

This workflow is how students finish degrees in 6-12 months instead of 2-4 years. Use our mentor tips to get your mentor on board with this approach.

Pre-OA Readiness Checklist

Before scheduling your OA, confirm you can check all these boxes:

  • Scored Competent or higher in ALL competency areas on PA
  • Took at least one PA without notes (simulating OA conditions)
  • Understand WHY correct answers are correct, not just WHAT they are
  • Reviewed any "Approaching Competent" areas thoroughly
  • Set up your proctoring environment
  • Scheduled OA within 2-3 days of your last strong PA (don't let knowledge fade)
