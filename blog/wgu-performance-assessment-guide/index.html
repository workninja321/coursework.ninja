WGU Performance Assessments: Write Papers That Pass First Time

December 17, 2025 10 min read Exam Preparation

Performance Assessments (PAs) are written assignments that demonstrate your mastery of course competencies. Unlike Objective Assessments which are multiple-choice exams, PAs require you to write papers, create projects, or complete practical tasks that show you can apply what you've learned.

For many WGU students, PAs can be intimidating—especially if you haven't written academic papers in years. But with the right approach, you can consistently pass PAs on your first submission. This guide shows you how.

Understanding WGU Performance Assessments

Before diving into strategies, let's understand what makes PAs unique:

  • Rubric-based evaluation: Every PA has a detailed rubric that tells you exactly what evaluators are looking for
  • Competency-focused: You're demonstrating that you can apply knowledge, not just recall it
  • No proctoring: PAs are completed on your own time, giving you flexibility
  • Unlimited revisions: If you don't pass, you can revise and resubmit
  • Originality checked: All submissions are checked for plagiarism

Master the Rubric First

The rubric is your blueprint for success. Every PA rubric breaks down exactly what competent, proficient, and exemplary work looks like. Here's how to use it:

Read the Rubric Before the Course Material

This might sound backwards, but reading the rubric first tells you what to focus on when studying the course material. You'll know exactly what concepts matter most for the assessment.

Pro Tip: Print the rubric and highlight every action verb (explain, analyze, describe, recommend). These verbs tell you exactly what the evaluator expects you to do.

Understand the Competency Levels

WGU rubrics typically have three levels:

  • Does Not Meet: Missing elements or insufficient depth
  • Competent: Meets all requirements—this is your target
  • Exemplary: Goes above and beyond (nice to have, not required)

Focus on meeting "Competent" for every rubric aspect. You don't need exemplary work to pass—you need complete work that addresses every requirement.

Analyze the Task Thoroughly

Before writing a single word, spend time understanding exactly what's being asked:

1 Read the Task Overview

Understand the scenario or context. Many PAs put you in a role (consultant, manager, analyst) and give you a realistic business scenario.

2 List Every Requirement

Go through each rubric section and list what you need to include. Create a checklist you can reference while writing.

3 Identify Word Count Expectations

Some PAs specify length requirements. If not mentioned, aim for thorough coverage—usually 2-4 paragraphs per major rubric section.

4 Note Citation Requirements

Check if you need to cite sources and which citation format to use (APA is most common at WGU).

Writing Strategies That Work

Use Section Headers That Match the Rubric

Make it easy for evaluators to find your work. If the rubric has sections A, B, C, D—use those same headers in your paper. This shows organization and ensures you don't miss anything.

Address Every Sub-requirement

If a rubric section asks you to "explain X and provide an example," make sure you do both. Missing even small sub-requirements is the most common reason for PA returns.

Common Mistake: Many students lose points by only partially addressing requirements. If the rubric asks for "three recommendations with justifications," you need exactly three recommendations AND a justification for each one.

Be Specific and Use Examples

Generic responses don't demonstrate competency. Instead of writing "communication is important for leaders," write "effective leaders use active listening and clear feedback to build trust with their teams, as demonstrated when..."

Connect to Course Material

Show that you've engaged with the course content. Reference specific concepts, theories, or frameworks from the course material. This demonstrates learning, not just good writing skills.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the scenario: If the PA gives you a business scenario, your responses must relate to that scenario—not generic examples
  • Over-relying on direct quotes: Evaluators want to see your understanding in your own words. Use quotes sparingly and always explain them
  • Ignoring formatting requirements: Follow any specified formatting guidelines (font, margins, headers) exactly
  • Submitting without proofreading: Grammar and spelling errors look unprofessional. Use tools like Grammarly or have someone review your work
  • Missing the word count: If a minimum is specified, meet it. If your response is too short, you probably haven't provided enough depth

The Revision Process

If your PA is returned for revision, don't panic. Here's how to handle it:

  1. Read the evaluator feedback carefully: Evaluators provide specific comments on what's missing or needs improvement
  2. Address every point mentioned: Don't just fix one thing and resubmit. Address all feedback
  3. Highlight your changes: Some students find it helpful to use a different color for revised text so they can easily review their changes
  4. Use your Course Instructor: If feedback is unclear, schedule a call with your CI for clarification
Success Rate: Most students who carefully address all evaluator feedback pass on their second submission. The key is thoroughly responding to every piece of feedback, not rushing the revision.

Time Management for PAs

PAs give you flexibility, but that freedom can lead to procrastination. Here's a framework for managing your time:

  • Day 1: Read the rubric and task. List all requirements. Begin course material review
  • Days 2-3: Complete course material focused on PA requirements
  • Day 4: Create an outline matching the rubric structure
  • Days 5-6: Write your first draft
  • Day 7: Edit, proofread, and submit

This timeline can be compressed if you're accelerating through your degree, but always allow time for revision before submission.

What Evaluators Look For

Understanding the evaluator's perspective helps you write better PAs:

  • Completeness: Did you address every rubric requirement?
  • Depth: Did you provide sufficient detail and explanation?
  • Application: Did you apply concepts to the given scenario?
  • Originality: Is this your own work in your own words?
  • Professionalism: Is your writing clear, organized, and error-free?
