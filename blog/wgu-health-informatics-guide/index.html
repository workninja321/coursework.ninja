WGU Health Informatics Degree: Bridge Healthcare and Technology

January 3, 2026 12 min read Degree Guides

Health informatics sits at the intersection of healthcare delivery, data management, and technology systems. WGU's B.S. Health Information Management prepares you to manage electronic health records, optimize clinical workflows, ensure regulatory compliance, and translate healthcare data into actionable insights that improve patient outcomes.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 15% job growth for health information technologists and specialists through 2034, significantly faster than the average for all occupations. With healthcare systems generating massive amounts of data and regulations like HIPAA requiring careful management, professionals who can bridge clinical operations and technology are in high demand.

WGU Health Information Management Program Overview

WGU's B.S. Health Information Management is designed for students who want to work in healthcare without direct patient care. The program is accredited by CAHIIM (Commission on Accreditation for Health Informatics and Information Management Education), which is the recognized accrediting body for HIM programs. This accreditation matters because it makes you eligible for the RHIA (Registered Health Information Administrator) certification through AHIMA.

The program falls under WGU's Leavitt School of Health and uses the same competency-based model as other WGU degrees. You advance by demonstrating mastery rather than sitting through a fixed semester schedule. If you already have experience in healthcare operations, medical coding, or clinical systems, you can move quickly through familiar content.

The Health Information Management curriculum covers healthcare data systems, medical terminology, pathophysiology, pharmacology, and the regulatory landscape that governs patient information. You learn how to manage health records throughout their lifecycle, from creation through storage, analysis, and secure disposal.

Tip: If you currently work in healthcare, document your access to EHR systems, reports, and workflows. Real examples from your workplace can accelerate your performance assessments and make your learning immediately applicable.

Key Systems and Technologies

Health informatics professionals work with a range of clinical and administrative systems. While WGU teaches concepts and workflows rather than vendor-specific training, understanding the major platforms helps you speak the language of healthcare IT.

  • Electronic Health Records (EHR): Epic, Cerner, Meditech, Allscripts, and athenahealth are common platforms
  • Revenue Cycle Systems: Claims management, denial tracking, and billing workflow tools
  • Clinical Decision Support: Alert systems, order sets, and evidence-based guidelines
  • Health Information Exchange (HIE): Interoperability platforms that share data across organizations
  • Data Warehouses: Analytics platforms for population health and operational reporting
  • Master Patient Index (MPI): Identity management systems that link patient records

Understanding Healthcare Data Standards

Data standards enable interoperability and consistent reporting across the healthcare ecosystem. As a health information manager, you will work with these standards regularly.

  • ICD-10-CM/PCS: Diagnosis and procedure coding for billing and reporting
  • CPT: Current Procedural Terminology for physician services
  • SNOMED CT: Clinical terminology for EHR documentation
  • LOINC: Laboratory and clinical observation identifiers
  • HL7 FHIR: Modern interoperability standard for data exchange
  • HIPAA Transaction Sets: Standardized formats for claims and eligibility

Career Paths and Salary Expectations

Health informatics careers span clinical, technical, and leadership roles. The field offers strong salaries and growth potential, particularly for professionals who can combine healthcare knowledge with data and technology skills.

According to recent salary data, Health Informatics Specialists earn an average of $91,323 annually, while Clinical Analysts earn around $80,252 per year. Leadership roles like Chief Information Officer (CIO) or Clinical Informatics Director can command six-figure salaries, often exceeding $150,000 in large health systems.

Industry Sectors Hiring Health Informatics Professionals

Health informatics skills are valued across the healthcare ecosystem, not just in hospitals. Consider these employment settings:

  • Health systems and hospitals: Largest employers, offering diverse roles from HIM to informatics leadership
  • Ambulatory clinics and physician practices: EHR management, billing optimization, quality reporting
  • Health insurance companies: Claims analysis, care management systems, data governance
  • Health IT vendors: Implementation consultants, product specialists, customer support
  • Government agencies: Public health informatics, Medicaid/Medicare systems, policy compliance
  • Consulting firms: Healthcare IT strategy, system selection, process improvement
  • Pharmaceutical and life sciences: Clinical trial data management, regulatory reporting

Acceleration Strategies for Health Informatics Students

Acceleration is about smart sequencing and leveraging your existing knowledge. Health informatics students with healthcare backgrounds often find they can move quickly through medical terminology, pathophysiology, and operational courses.

  1. Start with your strengths: If you work in healthcare, begin with courses that match your daily responsibilities.
  2. Front-load technical courses: Complete database, data analysis, and system courses while concepts are fresh.
  3. Use workplace examples: Document real EHR screens, reports, and workflows for performance assessments.
  4. Schedule assessments early: Book your exam dates to create accountability and maintain momentum.
  5. Batch related topics: Group compliance, privacy, and security courses together.
  6. Stay connected with your mentor: Ask them to open subsequent courses as soon as you complete one.
  7. Build a terminology reference: Healthcare has extensive jargon; keep a running glossary.
  8. Plan weekly study blocks: Consistency beats intensity for sustainable progress.
